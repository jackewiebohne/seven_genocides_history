Genocide and Perpetrators: A Historical Overview

Genocide and perpetrators

The definition of genocide adopted here by and large follows that of the UN Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide established in 1948. The concept of genocide and the legal convention are owing to the Polish-Jewish lawyer Raphael Lemkin who, suffering from Nazi persecution during WWII wrote a book that coined the notion to describe what was happening to the Jews and other groups.[1] He subsequently dedicated his life to enshrining “genocide” in international law and examined a large array of historical cases he considered genocide, most of which have subsequently been seriously discussed in genocide research. The UN Convention punishes:

any of the following acts committed with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnic, racial or religious group, as such:

(a) Killing members of the group;

(b) Causing serious bodily or mental harm to members of the group;

(c) Deliberately inflicting on the group conditions of life calculated to bring about its physical destruction in whole or in part;

(d) Imposing measures intended to prevent births within the group;

(e) Forcibly transferring children of the group to another group.[2]

It is a minor miracle the convention exists and I am convinced it has played an important role in bringing perpetrators to justice as well as, although much more indirectly, contributing to genocide prevention. Despite disagreements (often self-interested) by nations, actors, or individuals about which cases constitute genocide, it is difficult to find people who endorse committing genocide or reject the notion as useless or harmful. Having such a convention stipulated in international law makes the concept legally and politically actionable, and it plays an important role at international courts such as the International Court of Justice and, more importantly, the International Criminal Court as well as its quasi-subsidiaries established to try perpetrators of the Rwandan and Cambodian genocides as well as of the Bosnian war and genocide.

Despite its merits, we should remind ourselves that the convention is historically contingent. It was drafted with Joseph Stalin at the table, one of history’s worst mass murderers whose own atrocities would have been a clear case for the genocide convention if it had included political victims as Lemkin had envisioned it. Stalin, however, ensured that political and class-based victims did not become part of the convention.[3] On the other hand, we have a considerable overlap particularly between the groups categorized along “national, ethnic, racial” lines, and there is perhaps less but still significant overlap between these and “religious groups.” It is difficult to see how race-based persecution is not simply a subclass of a somewhat more broadly conceived ethnicity-based persecution. These overlaps and omissions indicate the political priorities of the parties at table when the convention was drafted: race and nationhood are protected, particularly against the onslaught of a foreign invader, but other forms of mass killing and conflict as well as political or economic motivations underlying atrocities are downplayed or ignored.[4] Because of these difficulties and contingencies, I draw on the convention more as a guideline than a law, and I have no trouble extending the notion of genocide to political and economic groups, both of which play an exceptionally important role in the Indonesian and Cambodian genocides. I will also dedicate more space to the question of gender, both of perpetrators and victims, than the UN Convention which has very little to say on this matter.

Other aspects of the convention also merit problematization. Historically and legally proving the requisite “intent to destroy” is exceedingly difficult and has received enormous attention.[5] For virtually any genocide covered in here one can find various more or less historically serious challenges as to whether it fits definitions of genocide, and the majority of these challenges build their case on the supposed insufficient proof of intent by high-level actors.[6] With few exceptions that I discuss just below, the intent to destroy in the cases covered here is sufficiently established in the historical (or legal) literature to constitute genocide and the majority of documentaries on each genocide do not dispute that. Despite the difficulties posed by the requirement to prove “intent” in the legal and historical domains, discussion of intent will play a somewhat different role here and focus on the question of “why” did perpetrators participate in violence, and how documentaries represent this “why”, rather than whether they committed the violence with the requisite, demonstrable categories of intent.[7]

Investigations into the reasons why perpetrators commit genocide are as old as the concept itself. However, only after a surprising amount of time elapsed did historians, psychologists, and, somewhat later, social or political scientists investigate the different causes, material and psychological, for participation in genocidal violence and scrutinize a wide scope of different perpetrators. Documentaries, as we will see in the next chapters, typically followed these trends with some lag. For a long time, the study of genocidal perpetrators was simply the study of Holocaust perpetrators. Beginning in the 1960s, coinciding with and partially sparked by the capture and trial of Holocaust perpetrator Adolf Eichmann, studies by psychologist Stanley Milgram, philosopher Hannah Arendt, and historian Raul Hilberg all, although in vastly different ways, showed how ordinary people could form part of a project of extreme violence. Milgram’s social psychological experiment showed how ordinary people, on the instructions of a supposed doctor, were dutifully willing to inflict ostensibly excruciating pain and dangerous injuries at the press of a button to a complete stranger (who was in fact an actor and not harmed). Milgram’s 1963 experiment provided a key explanation why perpetrators, at least of lower rank, may commit genocide on the command an instruction of an authority, with only a remarkably few being disobedient.[8] Arendt’s report on the Eichmann trial provided perhaps the most lasting catchphrase in the study of perpetrators, namely that the “evil” behind high-level bureaucrat and key architect in the implementation of the “Final Solution,” such as Eichmann, was “banal” in that it was not motivated by an exceptional hatred for Jews but rather dutiful bureaucratic pedantry.[9] Arendt turned out to be wrong in the case of Eichmann who was in fact deeply antisemitic,[10] but the concept was undoubtedly useful precisely because Eichmann at least partially served as a template, and as such it could be generalized and apply to others. A larger problem was that historical data and analysis of perpetrators, in Arendt and contemporary historians, was relatively thin. Raul Hilberg’s 1961 three-volume investigation into the bureaucratic apparatus of Nazi-Germany’s extermination of Jews was arguably the first comprehensive study of the Holocaust and its perpetrators,[11] and it has remained a major reference work to this day.

Despite these important early beginnings, studies of lower-level perpetrators, those who killed face-to-face, rather than bureaucratic distance, was arguably only ignited with Christopher Browning’s seminal 1992 work on German shooting squads in the earlier phases of the Holocaust. Examining demographic, situational, and ideological aspects, he analysed how a group of middle-aged policemen without extensive antisemitic ideological conditioning for mass murder came to murder through a web of factors involving group pressure and authority.[12] The wars and genocides in Bosnia and Rwanda in the mid-1990s furnished perpetrator studies with new examples and an unusual richness of a wide variety of data. Particularly in the case of Rwanda, political and social scientists now joined alongside historians, psychologists, and anthropologists in the study of perpetrators. Some of the strongest recent work on the causes and dynamics of perpetration on all levels, ranging from the highest to the lowest rung of the perpetrator hierarchy, comes from these newer contributions and the more recently joined disciplines equipped with a rich qualitative and quantitative toolset.[13] Near contemporaneous and extended international trials on the Rwandan genocide—paired with an unprecedented domestic Rwandan effort to bring perpetrators to justice—on the wars in former Yugoslavia, and, quite belatedly, the Cambodian genocide have made a wide range of different perpetrators, their testimonies, and mediatized faces accessible to a broad public to an heretofore unseen degree.

Documentaries have reflected, and occasionally anticipated or contributed to these wider developments in engaging with perpetrators. In so doing, documentaries’ identification of who the perpetrators are is generally quite conservative. In the majority of cases, perpetrators appearing in documentaries have been charged or already convicted in some form, be it at highly formalized international tribunals or the grassroots village justice of the Rwandan Gacaca. The reasons for this conservatism are relatively apparent. Documentaries are (mostly) safe from publicly and falsely branding potential innocents when they approach convicted perpetrators for testimonies, but incipient, ongoing, or completed legal processes also more generally indicate that documentaries can film witnesses, survivors, bystanders, or even non-charged perpetrators with greater safety in contexts of transitional justice than if perpetrators roamed with impunity—as we will see in the case of Indonesia where any engagement with perpetrators faces the greatest obstacles for these reasons. Yet, apart from Indonesia, a number of documentaries stand out in who they identify as perpetrators or in complicating the typical binaries between perpetrators and victims. They range from a 1960 German television documentary that interviews low-ranking former Waffen SS, then freely meeting in veterans’ associations all over Germany, several Cambodian documentaries (mostly since the turn of the millennium) featuring perpetrator encounters with survivors or perpetrators who were minors during their participation in the genocide, to a 2017 Danish documentary following a convicted Bosnian Muslim perpetrator attempting to apologize to his victims all the while fellow Bosnian Muslims tell him that since Bosnian Serbs committed the genocide it is Serbs who should apologize first.

At their best, documentaries paint complex pictures of perpetrators as human beings and the socio-political contexts in which they committed genocide. But even when documentaries aren’t at their best, more extensive representations of perpetrators can facilitate better understanding of the contexts extremist ideologues seize upon to secure power and drive an exterminatory agenda or in which ordinary men—and if there is a strong predictor of perpetration it is its maleness—become rank-and-file mass murderers even when they may have had sufficient freedom to choose otherwise. Beyond a focus on particular perpetrators, documentaries can also shed light, more abstractly, on the different patterns and dynamics of perpetration that should not merely be conflated with any particular individual. In all but one case—Indonesia being the exception—the genocides covered here were committed or reached their peak during wars, and wars confront perpetrators with the extreme life-or-death circumstances that let them imagine entire groups as enemies (and even in Indonesia many perpetrators claim to have found themselves, however erroneously, in a war-like kill-or-be-killed situation). Yet, what emerges from documentaries with sufficient historical nuance, such as Laurence Rees’s two major docuseries on the Holocaust,[14] is that genocide is not an inevitable outcome driven by single-minded, monolithic extremists bent upon destruction, but rather that political expedience, economic needs, military failures, or even tensions between different perpetrator groups accelerate, decelerate, or even halt extermination programs, expand or contract victim categories, and commission or decommission perpetrator groups.

In the following chapters we take a closer look at how documentaries represent perpetrators of all ranks and, more abstractly perpetration, as well as how such representations vary across time and genocide. To put readers on a solid footing about perpetrators and perpetration of each of the genocides covered here, I expound on these in the following in further detail.

 

The Herero and Nama genocide

The Herero and Nama genocide is probably the least known of the genocides covered here. The Herero and Nama are two ethnic groups in today’s Namibia who played a particularly pronounced role in pre-colonial and colonial Namibia when it was colonized by Wilhelmine Germany during the “scramble for Africa” in the 1880s. The Herero, who then constituted the majority ethnic group in the territory, and Nama were nomadic, living largely off cattle, given that climate and soil made agricultural exploitation of the terrain exceedingly difficult. Although the Nama and Herero competed over land and cattle and frequently clashed violently, the Herero and Nama leaders Samuel Maharero and Henrik Witbooi, respectively, negotiated a peace in 1892 in recognition that the Germans posed the greater threat (although Maharero would later form alliances with Germans to quell intra-Herero opposition to his rule).[15]

Colonizers across Africa brought with them the rinderpest which eventually, in 1896, reached what the Germans called German South West Africa and killed Herero and Nama cattle in scores. Despite somewhat haphazard inoculation efforts by the colonizers, the mass death of their cattle deprived the Herero and Nama of vital nutrition and livelihoods. The epizootic and the famines and natural disasters that followed in its wake had an enormously profound impact on the native societies and provided opportunities for colonizer encroachment on their lands that could now, emptied of cattle and value, be cheaply bought, or simply taken, from impoverished, weakened natives.[16]

If the rinderpest destroyed the Herero economy, routine settler violence and discrimination against non-whites threatened their lives and culture, and thus rumours of a potential Herero uprising among the Germans and a trigger-happy German officer seems to have ignited a localized Herero uprising in 1904 in which soon all Herero participated.[17] The Herero were armed, knew the terrain, far exceeded the Germans in number, and conducted their military operations initially successfully. When the Germans realized that the Herero would not fall easily despite perceived German military superiority, the German Emperor and German army high command (the so-called General Staff) involved themselves, sending troops, materiel, and general Lothar von Trotha who had already proven his capabilities in colonial violence when he helped suppress the Boxer rebellion in China. Trotha, in line with typical German army operations, sought to encircle and defeat the Herero in one single large battle at the Waterberg mountain. Yet, after an indecisive exchange of fire, the Herero broke through the encircling German army lines that were weakest in the Omaheke desert. Trotha fearing failure issued his “extermination order” (Vernichtungsbefehl) which commanded all Herero men to be executed and all women and children to be pursued into the desert.[18] Continued deadly German raids into the desert and blocking access to water holes, ensured that only small groups of Herero survived the trek through the desert beyond German reach. The extermination order was soon revoked, nonetheless, the lethal desert raids continued.[19] Settlers and local newspapers called for the war to be led with explicit disregard for humanitarian mercy and conceived of the war as “race war,” an assessment shared by Trotha and German army high command in Berlin.[20] These were at times opposed in voice and action by German missionaries.[21] That particularly strong racist tendencies played a role in the escalation of the war and subsequent treatment of the Herero and, later, Nama can also be seen by the fact that in 1905 anti-miscegenation laws were passed in the colony, followed thereafter by similar laws in other German colonies—behaviour in which Germans were an outlier among European colonizers at the time.[22] The remaining few Herero who did not manage to trek to adjacent, non-German-occupied territories, and who managed to survive the extermination campaign until the establishment of prisoner-of-war camps often surrendered to German authorities. They would linger for months or years in concentration camps where further thousands died, already exhausted from the war, under appalling conditions and forced labour. The official military report compiled by and for the authorities in the aftermath of the Herero genocide concludes its assessment of the German penal campaign (Strafgericht) by noting soberly that it had found its end when the Herero had ceased to exist as a people.[23]

The Nama entered into war with the Germans one day after Trotha’s extermination order against the Herero. Until his decommissioning and return to Germany in 1905, Trotha shifted his focus and forces to this theatre of war. The Nama, however, were tactically particularly sophisticated opponents whose small, swift formations escaped the large-scale encirclement operations typical for German warfare. Only after Trotha’s replacement, Berthold von Deimling formed small, agile units that could pursue Nama formations with equal speed, did the Germans achieve military and lethal successes.[24] The Nama were offered terms of surrender under which they would relinquish all arms and would have to turn themselves and their entire families in for imprisonment in the concentration camps that had already been erected to intern the few Herero survivors for the same purposes. That the Herero were the target of an annihilation campaign conducted under general Lothar von Trotha is, by now, uncontroversial.[25] The case for the Nama is less clear. The military campaign against them was extraordinarily severe but did not pursue an annihilatory intention or execution to the same extent as the Herero.[26] Most Nama deaths occurred in prisoner-of-war camps, established for the Herero and Nama peoples and maintained even after von Trotha’s decommissioning. Their lethal nature, however, is the subject of debate.

In the most comprehensive book on the camps to date, Jonas Kreienbaum has argued convincingly that they were not constructed with the explicit intent to destroy, that is their overriding purpose was not extermination.[27] There is evidence of “good” intentions—in relative terms—on part of the main civilian authority (Friedrich von Lindequist) that later took over the administration of the camps[28] or the occasional military commander, such as Berthold von Deimling who dissolved the most lethal camp at Shark Island[29] or Colonel Dame who opposed the mistreatment of prisoners.[30] These complicate arguments that the camps were genocidal. However, intentions and policies in genocide are more complex than typical public narratives of collectively acting perpetrators with longstanding evil intentions  that they mechanistically execute. Mass death of the entire imprisoned Herero and Nama, should it come about, was an acceptable outcome in the eyes of the civilian authority Oskar Hintrager,[31] and there was even a direct public call to destroy the Nama by the editor of the main German newspaper in South West Africa,[32] which suggests that a public imaginary of extermination existed and could be voiced openly without censure. These were not the only voices, however. Civilian and military authorities disagreed over the continuation of the camps after “pacification” with the former in favor, the latter against.[33] But sophisticated approaches to mass violence and comparative genocide acknowledge and accommodate conflictual opinions between and contradictory deeds by different perpetrator groups. Even in the Holocaust with its supposedly exemplary degree of planning and destruction,[34] for example, there were conflicts and contradictions between ghetto authorities, SS, police, military, and the civilian administration of the Nazi-occupied Polish territories of the so-called General Government,[35] especially in the case of Lodz ghetto.[36]

Whatever the particular intentions of German public figures in colonial Namibia, we already noted that settlers (and high-ranking military) often perceived the war as a racial one. It is also patently clear that, notwithstanding the differences of opinion, the extreme death rates in the camps were a more acceptable outcome than the risks, largely imagined given the extent to which especially the Herero but also the Nama had already been destroyed, of renewed wars with any freed Herero and Nama population. Even accounting for the limitations of medical knowledge at the time (e.g. how to treat vitamin deficiencies), the lethal outcomes were foreseeable, certainly after months and years of high camp mortality. The death rates in the camps from exposure, malnutrition, disease, overwork, and abuse were truly staggering. While data on the camps’ death rates are incomplete, the possible range of the death rate is between 31% and 64% of prisoners (but for various reasons a higher-than 31% death toll is likelier).[37] This makes them more lethal than contemporary Boer concentration camps in British South Africa and comparably lethal or even more lethal than non-exterminatory concentration camps under the Nazis for that matter (and comparably lethal to Nazi prisoner-of-war camps for Red Army soldiers, which I argue below, should form an essential part of the study of Nazi mass violence).[38]

The reasons for the exterminatory consequences of the camps for the Herero and Nama are manifold. (1) The camps targeted entire peoples: During the war, the Herero and Nama were – even if with varying fervor – hunted down and imprisoned after a war that was de facto waged against their entire nation. Even if the purpose of annihilating the Nama in the war was not as explicit as in the case of the Herero,[39] both nations’ armies were predominantly accompanied by their families and carried their entire possessions and economic means (especially cattle). Any military campaign thus carried enormous risks to civilians from the outset, which was further aggravated by the particularly merciless form of colonial warfare against an asymmetric opponent whose population was not subject to the same cultural or legal norms.[40] German victories meant pillaging, often accompanied by rape and murder, as a matter of routine. Economic and reproductive means to sustain the populations were thus severely affected by German warfare. The wholesale targeting of the peoples changed little when it came to imprisonment which sought the incarceration of Hereros and Namas to as complete an extent as possible. [41] The only threat the Herero posed after the extermination campaign was cattle theft carried out for their own survival,[42] yet in the German colony they faced the indiscriminate imprisonment of their entire nation because of the supposed threat these peoples, not just able-bodied men, posed to security. Tellingly, a colonial postcard from South West Africa labelled “native prisoners of war” shows Nama with a substantial number of children in the front row.[43] What exact intentions led to the classification of entire peoples as prisoners-of-war by the Germans is somewhat unclear. The overall context of colonial racism very likely played a significant role here: In British-colonized South Africa the military men of the white Boers were interned separately from the camps where women and children were held.[44] Large-scale deaths in the German camps thus meant not “only” the deaths of persons, but peoples.

(2) Inadequate access to vital resources: Prison camps are extremely controlled environments, “total institutions.”[45]  In such an environment, direct killing, the intentional withholding of resources, or careless neglect have identical consequences, thus mass deaths in such institutions should be (and legally are) considered differently from mass deaths in complex polities and economies encompassing millions of people where the lethal consequences of neglect and incompetence (such as in the famine of Mao’s Great Leap China)[46] are more difficult to determine than camps. In the case of German-run concentration camps in today’s Namibia, the extreme lethality of the camps had been reported on already two months into their establishment in 1905.[47] Several of especially the biggest camps were adjacent to cities,[48] conditions and deaths were thus often quite public. Furthermore, the dying happened over the sustained period of roughly two years (from the end of 1905 until the mid-1907 but in some cases until spring 1908). The causes of mortality – starvation, disease, exposure, and physical abuse – were as evident as preventable, not least by simply foregoing the wholesale imprisonment of peoples. The undersupply of material is at times truly astonishing, such as the provision of 80 blankets for 1200 to 1500 prisoners.[49] Nutrition, health services, and the provision of vital goods varied over time and camps, and attempts were made to remedy the high mortality in the camps. Ultimately, however, where British authorities interning Boers in (near contemporaneous) South African concentration camps sought to and succeeded in manifestly improving death rates in their camps, the Germans did too little, too late, or even resisted improving conditions so that the camps did not lose their punitive character.[50]

(3) Death and labor as punishment: Even though the camps were first administered by the military and then civilian authorities, they never lost their punitive character. Military authorities had at times explicitly considered suffering and deaths due to imprisonment as part of the punishment.[51] There is somewhat less evidence this was the case for the civilian authorities, but evidence does exist.[52] The punitive and lethal character of the camps remained effectively until the camps closed. The punitive character, however, also had economic aspects. Only the status of the camps as institutions of punishment and the Hereros’ and Namas’ status as prisoners (and thus, ultimately, enemies) made it possible to use them for forced labor, since slavery was officially abolished in German colonies.[53] Even the sick, elderly, and children from age five were forced to work, in some cases work continued through the night with increasingly no resting days granted,[54] and severe corporal punishment was extended to women and children.[55] Rapes occurred frequently in the camps.[56] Rapes by German soldiers at the frontlines had already led to an absolutely astonishing venereal disease rate given by military medical reports of 93.3% (but numbers include multiple infections).[57] Women were officially exempt from corporeal punishment and rapes were not sanctioned, but paradoxically, considering the government ban on racial intermarriage, the ethnic othering of the prisoners meant there was little to no incentive to enforce the rules—for the consequences would have been to punish a white man for what he did to a supposed racial inferior who themselves was interned precisely for purposes of punishment—and the administrative and institutional infrastructure to do so was lacking in any case.[58]

When the wars were over and the last camp dissolved in 1908 by releasing its prisoners, approximately only a third of the Herero population and half the original Nama population remained alive.[59] Indeed, although the data are limited in number and accuracy, they are nonetheless unambiguously indicative of the severe demographic impact of German colonial rule on the native population, causing a 23-35% depopulation of the territory, more severe than any other colonial rule.[60] To this day, the Herero numbers have not recovered to constitute the majority ethnicity they once were. Perpetrators were not prosecuted. To the contrary, Trotha received high military honours for his slaughtering upon his 1905 return to Germany.

 

The Armenian genocide

For centuries Armenians within the Ottoman Empire had, as other religious minorities, enjoyed relative freedom and a degree of self-governance. This was owing to the so-called Millet system, which enabled confessional communities to self-legislate as long as they paid taxes to the Ottoman state. This changed with the onset of national awakenings among the Turks, Armenians, and other minorities within the Ottoman Empire that furthermore showed clear signs of ossification and senility. The Ottoman Empire began rapidly shedding territories in the second half of the 18th century, losing parts of the Caucasus, Anatolia, and the Balkans, in a war with Russia in which Russia had supported nationalists in the Balkans in the name of pan-Slavism.[61] The Ottoman Empire thus began looking more inward towards its Anatolian heartlands that were also claimed as the heartlands of Ottoman-Armenians and Ottoman-Kurds.[62] Almost contemporaneously, the 1864 Russian genocide of at least 600,000 Circassian Muslims at the Empire’s border meant an influx of a minimum of 600,000 refugees that were to be settled within the Empire’s borders.[63] Over the next decades population dynamics would continue to be volatile and this would not be the last time that large numbers of Muslim refugees bringing tales of atrocities that were also perceived along religious lines, given that the Russians were Christians, arrived in the Ottoman Empire; many Circassians would participate in the genocide of Christian Armenians several decades later.[64]

When in Anatolia of the 1890s tensions rose up between Armenians and Kurds that played out along ethno-national and religious lines but were also caused by centuries of militant Kurdish domination over and deprivation of the Armenians in the region, Ottoman troops (Hamidye) together with local Kurds broke any local Armenian resistance through indiscriminate massacres that cost the lives of between 100,000 and 300,000 Armenians (including through indirect causes like disease and starvation), but also included several thousand Assyrian Christians and Greek.[65] We will see lines of continuity between the Hamidian massacres, including the targeting of multiple Christian minorities in subsequent bouts of anti-Armenian persecution and strong geographic overlap between the locations of the Hamidian massacres and the most lethal locations for Armenians during genocide.[66] From the point of view of the sultan at the time, Abdülhamid II, who had ordered the repression, the massacres worked. Anatolian Kurds, who had historically posed some obstacles to Ottoman rule, had allied with Ottoman-Turks and many had been integrated into the Hamidian regiments, while Armenians refrained from further resistance.[67] It seemed, the wholesale destruction of peoples that betrayed a hint of trouble, particularly if they were Christian, had proven to be a successful recipe for the future.

Although the Ottoman state was able to quell, for the moment, internal problems, its inability to address systemic issues such as its bankrupt administration and an obsoleted army structure meant that it continued to crumble at its peripheries. Over several decades it lost territories to major European powers (Britain, France, Italy) in Northern Africa (1880s until 1910s). Eventually the enormous challenges faced by the Ottoman Empire proved to be too big to avoid a transformation of power. For several years a pan-Turkic nationalist movement (Ittihadism) had begun to grow that postulated that Turks, like Europeans, were Caucasian and had migrated eastward from their Central Asian home, but mixed its pan-Turkic social Darwinist theses with a pronounced Islamist rhetoric, which despite its beliefs in the superiority of Turkic “race” above other Muslim groups in the Empire did not abandon the idea of Muslim unity.[68] The political activists espousing this ideology called themselves Young Turks and organized themselves as the so-called “Committee of Union and Progress” (CUP). In 1908 the Committee staged a revolution forcing the sultan to reinstate the parliament and cede power which was headed by three figures who all played an extraordinary role in the Armenian genocide a few years later: Talaat, Mehmet Nâzım Bey (known as Dr Nâzım), and Bahaeddin Şakir.[69] The revolution suggested better times for the various minorities, including the Armenians, who now enjoyed equal status and the right to become politically active, enabling them to pursue their own national agenda out in the open. Yet, the revolution did nothing to establish more political stability in and in 1909 a counterrevolution by reactionary forces temporarily unseated the Young Turks. This counterrevolution in turn prompted local Muslims in the Adana region of the Empire, where a substantial Christian minority now with equal status seemed to threaten Muslim interests. A complex mixture of local administrators stemming from the old regime, local Muslim villagers, Muslim refugees having fled encroachments of Christian European powers on Ottoman territories, but also supporters of the Young Turks were responsible for the 1909 massacres of 20,000 to 30,000 Armenians and over a thousand other Christians in Adana. [70] The slaughtering was ended by Young Turk sympathetic forces that returned the Committee of Union and Progress to its seat in power and now fully deposed the sultan Abdülhamid II to replaced him with Mehmet V who would reign, mostly in name only for most power lay with the Young Turks, until the end of WWI.

Continued tensions and political instability within were joined one more by external ones. When in 1912 war broke out in the Balkans, the Ottoman Empire and its successor Turkey found themselves in a state of war that would effectively continue in some form until Kemal Attatürk won the Turkish independence wars and became Turkey’s first president in 1923. The wars in the Balkans (1912-13), where Muslims made up about a third of the local population,[71] were particularly violent and traumatic for the Ottoman Empire, since the loss of the territories meant the Ottoman Empire lost effectively all of its European possessions. Although none of the war parties behaved with much restraint towards noncombatants, the complete defeat of the Ottoman Empire in the war meant that local Muslims bore the brunt of Ottoman incompetence. Once again, tens of thousands and possibly up to several hundreds of thousands of Muslims were slaughtered, and a further 500,000 to 600,000 brutalized refugees fled or were expelled to the Ottoman Empire where they were to be settled in Anatolia.[72] The geographic and demographic center of gravity in the Ottoman Empire decidedly shifted away from Christianity to Islam.

The Balkan wars also brought to the fore the military wing of the Committee, the shadowy Special Organization (Teşkilât-ı Mahsusa) which consisted of military officers, highwaymen, released criminals, and also included Kurdish units of the former Hamidian regiments.[73] The influential Committee members Nâzım and Şakir both came from the Balkans, were politically radicalized by the Balkan wars, and were leading figures in the Special Organization.[74]

On the eve of the Ottoman entry into WWI the organizations, structures, and ideologies that would produce the Armenian genocide were in place. Insecurity from external enemies was met with a radicalization of politics, while internal volatility in a continuously shrinking Ottoman Empire forced to concentrate on its core lands was met with extreme violence that past precedent had shown was a successful means of controlling populations. Young Turk social Darwinist ideology had hardened the boundaries between the different ethnic groups, making the different non-Turkic “races” particularly within the Turkic core lands increasingly undesirable. Armenian nationalism that claimed much of the same land for itself as the Young Turks’ self-conceived Turkic nation was a seemingly especially acute political threat, was ideologically opposed, and, important for the Young Turks but arguably even more so for local rank-and-file perpetrators of the genocide, had different religious beliefs. The importance of this religious difference was amplified by the fact that many of the same Christian powers continually impinging on the Ottoman borders acted as spokesmen for Armenian nationalist demands for an independent Armenia or also intervened diplomatically when Armenians within the Empire were attacked.[75] This seemed to reproduce the external Ottoman-Christian country enmities within Ottoman borders as Muslim-Christian enmity.

Historic Armenia, situated in the Caucasus and split between the Russian and Ottoman Empire proved to be the location of the greatest Ottoman disaster in the early years of WWI. Russia invading with a modern army that included Russian-Armenian volunteers and Ottoman-Armenian defectors—mobilized in part because of atrocities by the Ottoman army passing through Russian-Armenian villages (though Russians did the same to Muslims on their side of the border)[76]—on the Russian side delivered a crushing defeat to the Ottoman Empire at the battle of Sarıkamış that effectively destroyed an entire Ottoman army in January 1915.[77] Deserting Armenians were the convenient pretext and historic violence against Armenians the context for what would now evolve into a policy of genocide. Already in February after the defeat Armenian regiments in the Ottoman army were disarmed. Sometime around March and early April, as France and Britain began a major offensive (in Gallipoli) directly threatening Constantinople, Şakir was in close communication with the Committee of Union and Progress and the decision to destroy Armenians was taken, a task with which the Special Organization was entrusted.[78] What followed, roughly in sequence, the deportation and eventual execution of leading civilian Armenian figures only further spurred by the armed rebellion of Armenians in Van (Eastern Anatolia) whose uprising in turn was a reaction to widened anti-Armenian atrocities in the Van region.[79] Thereafter Armenian men were gathered and then killed followed by the mass deportations of women and children who were usually forcibly marched eastward through the desert until they perished in today’s Syria. Women were raped beyond measure; sexual violence in female survivor testimony is nearly universal. Children were variously slaughtered or starved, but a remarkable number survived as orphans or were taken in by friendly Muslim families, which often meant conversion involving different degrees of coercion. Mass pillaging and looting accompanied deportations and killings, whether on the ground where the local perpetrators often were worse off than Armenians, or by the high-level governmental seizure of Christian assets or factories (such wholesale pillaging at all levels would be witnessed again during the Holocaust).[80] Deportations and mass murder peaked in 1915 and 1916 and were particularly severe in Northern and Eastern Turkey where typically over 90% of local Armenians disappeared.[81] Somewhere between 664,000 and up to 1 million Armenians perished in the most lethal period of the genocide in 1915 to 1916, and over the thirty year period from the mid-1890s until the end of deportations in the mid-1920s at least a million Armenians had been murdered.[82]

As with the Holocaust, however, the Ottoman-Turkish murdering should be examined more comprehensively. Armenians were certainly the primary target of Muslim and Young Turk murder; their deportations had the largest geographic spread, peaked the earliest, and were the most lethal in both absolute and relative numbers.[83] Nonetheless, between 500,000 and 1 million Greeks and Assyrian Christians were murdered and well over a million deported from just before WWI until the consolidation and independence of modern Turkey in 1924.[84] Sometimes non-Armenian Christians deportations and murder happened as “collateral damage” during anti-Armenian measures, but far more often non-Armenian Christians followed slightly different rationales albeit still fitting with the overall Young Turk goal of a more Turkish or at least more Islamic population in the central Turkish lands. In June 1914, on the cusp of WWI, 100,000-200,000 Ottoman-Greeks were removed, with the keen help from Muslim refugees from the Balkans, in the sensitive Ottoman borderlands with Europe in June 1914.[85] During WWI, then, several hundreds of thousands of Greeks were deported or killed, but many of such removals were carried out in the vicinity of frontlines, whereas Armenians were exterminated in areas where Greeks far outnumbered them but were left untouched.[86] Yet, circumstances changed when, following a brief respite after Ottoman surrender in 1918, an opportunistic Greece invaded Turkey in 1919, which at that time seemed ripe for the taking with Italian, British, and French troops stationed in the country and peace treaties that anticipated the division of Turkey among the victors.[87] United under Mustafa Kemal who had proven his military abilities during WWI, the Turks fought a ferocious war of independence that also went hand in hand with the wholesale deportation, but also killing, of the Greek population, before the Greek military defeat and departure in 1923 led to an agreement between Turkey and Greece that foresaw a population exchange of up to 900,000 Ottoman-Greeks and 200,000 Greek Muslims.[88] During this phase, the remainder of the Armenians (if they were not children taken in by Muslims during 1915-16), who had no sovereign country to flee to, were killed, deported, or fled of their own accord (usually the Russian Caucasus region).[89]

Perpetrators came from all strata of society and included a vast number of willing civilian murderers from all ethnic Muslim groups in the Ottoman Empire who needed no coercion, peer pressure, or official orders to initiate or participate. This is in marked difference to the Holocaust where perpetrators were integrated into institutional frameworks and state organs that had a clearer monopoly of executive violence, such as police, paramilitary (SS), or military, although Ottoman military and gendarmes were important perpetrator groups during the Armenian genocide as well. Anti-Jewish pogroms, especially in Eastern Europe early during WWII with willing locals, existed of course,[90] but were very small relative to the proportions of official Nazi mass killing.[91] In this sense, the Ottoman and post-Ottoman mass murders between the 1890s reaching up to the mid-1920s were more similar to the Rwandan and Indonesian genocides (in the Herero and Nama genocide large numbers of settlers participated but mostly upon formal integration into regular forces). The most consistent and official, if very shadowy, organ of Armenian mass murder was the Special Organization that was composed, at the top level, of Turkish elites but on the ground shared the mosaic ethnic makeup of Muslim Ottomans, ranging from Balkan Muslims, Circassians, to Kurds and could involve former soldiers, brigands, and even criminals specifically released for rape and murder (in this they are reminiscent of the infamously cruel SS Dirlewanger brigade).[92] As in the Rwandan, but also perhaps to a lesser extent Cambodian and Indonesian genocides, the main means of murder was with sharp weapons or agricultural tools, and the methods were exuberantly sadistic.[93]

Several of high and mid-ranking perpetrators, including the three Pashas Talaat, Enver, and Cemal, as well as Nâzım and Şakir, among many others, were prosecuted and court martialed by interim Ottoman governments that anticipated international trials by the victorious allies. Several of the leading men were condemned to death but had already fled the country, while the court dragged its feet over reaching verdicts, or executing them, for those in custody, and the trials remained incomplete. Allied efforts to amass evidence for their own trials were not particularly vigorous and stopped, in any case, once Turkey had evicted allied occupation forces.[94] Thus, justice against leading figures of the Armenian genocide was left to Armenian avengers who managed to assassinate Talaat, Djemal, and Şakir, among others. The Trial, in turn, of Talaat’s assassin Soghomon Tehlirian in Germany curiously led to no conviction, despite the court’s having no doubt about the evidence for and deliberateness of the murder, when it came to light that Talaat was the main architect of the genocide (and that Germany, the most important Ottoman ally, had done nothing to intervene) and how severely the genocide had affected Tehlirian’s mental state.[95]

Comparably little about the perpetrators is known. Given a period of almost thirty years of lethal anti-Armenian persecution (and over a decade of anti-Greek persecution), there is considerable incongruence between policies and major perpetrators across this period. The first large-scale massacres were carried out under Sultan Abdülhamid, the most lethal under the triumvirate of Talaat, Enver, and Cemal Pasha with Talaat in the driving seat, and the last under Mustafa Kemal who would be known as Atatürk. This poses some challenges to painting a coherent picture of the perpetrators across this period. Perhaps the most formidable challenge to the study of the perpetrators is posed by the closure of crucial archives, those of the military and the Special Organization, or in any case have been purged altogether (including of critical evidence gathered during the postwar trials), in a denialist Turkey that built its foundation on the murder and cleansing of its Christian peoples and subsequently turned to the persecution of its Kurdish minority not considered part of Turkish identity.[96] Turkey’s denialist intransigence has not changed to this day.

 

The Holocaust

The Holocaust typically refers to the Nazi extermination of almost six million Jews during WWII. Although, the history of the Holocaust is relatively well-known, the more expansive view of the Nazis’ genocidal policies taken here, extending well beyond Jews, is worth covering in more detail here. Of the many groups targeted by the Nazis, Jews were indubitably the single most lethally persecuted in relative (well over 90% of European Jews) and absolute numbers, over the longest period of time (for German Jews effectively from 1933 until 1945), and the most expansively across all Nazi-occupied territories. Insofar as Jews posed no dangers whatever to Germans and were exterminated for reasons entirely imagined by the Germans, the genocide against the Jews is arguably unique among Nazi genocides and among perhaps all genocides as Yehuda Bauer has argued.[97] Yet, in my view, far more is gained than lost in seeing the genocide against the Jews not as a separate case but as the core aspect of a much larger-scale Nazi extermination program. This, in any case, is the enormously fruitful direction taken by a younger generation of Holocaust scholars.[98] Furthermore, much is gained by analyzing the Holocaust comparatively as another genocide among genocides,[99] or a mass killing among mass killings.[100] As we will see in the coming chapters, the Holocaust, for better or worse, is often the touchstone of memory and history, including documentary film, against which other genocides are measured.[101]

From the moment Hitler gained power, the Nazi-regime cost lives. Nonetheless, the type of targeting, lethality, and the scale of the violence changed substantially over time. The first concentration camp Dachau, near Munich, and other early camps, targeted political opponents, especially communists and socialists, in the initial phase of Nazi power consolidation. The inmates were subjected to arbitrary torture, forced labor, and were sometimes murdered. The racial dimension of such political persecution was already apparent in this early stage, as Jews among the incarcerated political opponents were killed in disproportionate numbers.[102] Nonetheless, outright race-based lethal persecution was to become the dominant hallmark of Nazi violence later. Perpetrators of these early murders were SS men who belonged to a hard core of early Nazi followers and were, contrary to many of the later perpetrators of Nazi mass killings, not “ordinary.” These murders followed traditions of rightwing political assassinations committed by the paramilitary Freikorps and the Nazi militia SA over a period of many years before the Nazi rise to power, and this early violence was “permissive, rather than prescriptive,” insofar as the murders were not organized and ordered from above but took place in an environment where it was acceptable.[103]

The concentration camp system was successively enlarged over the years, and the Nazis increasingly shifted their attention towards anti-Jewish persecution, not least with the passing of the Nuremberg Laws in 1935. Among many other things, these forbade intermarriage between Germans and Jews. The 1938 anti-Jewish pogrom (Reichskristallnacht) organized by the SS and SA was the first ostensibly and publicly visible act of violence against Jews, including lethal violence which resulted in the deaths of several hundreds of Jews through murder or suicide. However, the first systematic lethal targeting in large numbers by the Nazis occurred only in the context of war against Poland, in an atmosphere in which human life was more expendable, allowing for more radical implementations of Nazi ideology. In interconnected actions, the Nazis used special killing squads (Einsatzgruppen) to target Polish religious or political leaders, intellectuals, Jews, Romani, and people with disabilities,[104] while within Germany a program (“T4”) was underway to kill people with disabilities, including with poison gas, who were deemed “useless eaters” and posed a risk to the health of the German race.[105] These planned killings cost the lives of between 130,000 and 150,000 disabled people as well as several tens of thousands on Polish territory over the course of two years (throughout the Nazi reign possibly several hundreds of thousands), and they were executed by a hard core of long-serving Nazis.[106] Pseudo-biological and racial motives are apparent in these extermination plans even where they, as in the murder of the Polish leadership, seemed to serve political purposes of Nazi-power consolidation against an occupied nation; it is telling that atrocities of such scale and well behind frontlines occurred in the militant eastward expansion of the Nazi empire onto “Slavic” territory, not during the German conquest of Western Europe.

Both the Einsatzgruppen kill squads and leading figures of the euthanasia program, many of whom were doctors, would be critically involved in full-fledged mass murder of Jews. However, again only war facilitated Nazi mass murder of unprecedented proportions. The 1941 invasion of the communist Soviet Union, which the Nazis believed (entirely erroneously) to be run by Jews and which was considered the political arch enemy, in the largest military offensive in history, was to finally realize the Nazi ideal of a racially reorganized Europe and to secure agricultural and oil resources in the coming wars against the UK and US. In preparation for the war and securing the conquered territories, Einsatzgruppen kill squads were expanded and joined by order police, and other auxiliary units, including those composed of men from newly conquered territories (Ukrainians and Lithuanians in disproportionate numbers)[107] with orders to execute Jews, suspected partisans, and communist leaders among Soviet military (Commissars). Their leaders were economists, lawyers, professors or held doctorate titles (Otto Rasch even had two), the rank-and-file perpetrators, however, in many cases fit the description of Browning’s “ordinary men.” Within a few months, these units operating in the rear of the rapidly eastward advancing German army killed in the hundreds of thousands and soon systematically included women and children among their targets. Where no specific kill squads were assigned, such as in Belarus that was to be the most devastated country of all in the war, the German army took upon itself the task of mass murder.[108]

Although especially Jews were murdered by the hundreds of thousands in the first six months of the war, by far the largest victim group of Nazi policy were Soviet prisoners of war. If they were not shot outright, they were huddled together in camps, often open fields surrounded by barbed wire, left entirely to the elements, with effectively no provision of food or water. Two million were to die by early 1942 and up to three or 3.3 million by the end of the war.[109] They constitute the second largest victim group, after the Jews, of Nazi mass murder, yet to date no English-language monograph exists studying their fate. [110] Their deaths formed an integral part of a larger Nazi starvation program (Hungerplan), the brainchild of agronomist Herbert Backe, intending the deaths of up to thirty million “Slavs”, particularly populations of urban centers that were slated to be razed entirely to make way for their agricultural exploitation by the German overlords. The starvation plan largely failed because of difficulties of controlling the food distribution across the conquered Soviet territories, and thus Soviet prisoners of war, Jews in Polish ghettos, and the starvation of up to a million people in the siege of Leningrad (today’s St. Petersburg) constituted its primary victims.[111]

But the Nazis’ hopes and dreams of annihilatory occupation of Eastern Europe went beyond even the starvation plan. In a separate plan, running through drafts exhibiting ever increasing ambitions of mass murder, the Nazis envisioned the extermination, deportation, and enslavement of virtually the entirety of Eastern Europe. The final draft from spring 1943 of this so-called General Plan for the East (Generalplan Ost), devised primarily by the Reich Security Main Office (RSHA) that oversaw all German security forces (from the SS to the police), hoped to administer the deaths of 11.3 million Jews, based on the Wannsee numbers, and 17 million Slavs with a further 31 million Slavs to be deported to Siberia and the remainder reduced to slavery.[112] In short, the Nazis “proposed destruction on a scale never before contemplated in human history.” [113] Despite having suffered under one of history’s greatest mass murderers, Stalin, and despite the destruction caused by the Nazis’ war, it is a remarkable and underappreciated fact that the people of Eastern Europe would have fared even worse after the war had the Nazis won it.[114]

Both plans, only very partial in their execution and with overlapping goals and target victims, arose directly out a racist ideology that was most lethally targeted at Jews. Indeed, as we can see in the last draft of the General Plan for the East, the genocide against the Jews was the primary objective but placed alongside a larger genocidal program for Eastern Europeans. Ideologically, Hitler had linked Jews and Bolshevism as well as regarded “Slavic” Eastern Europe as integral for the German quest for living space the mid-1920s already.[115] Similarly, especially in regions where partisan resistance was particularly pronounced such as Yugoslavia and, even more so, Belarus, Jews were often synonymous with partisans.[116] Thus, the murder of Jews and the mass targeting of civilians, the burning and razing of entire villages that may have cost the lives of perhaps one million non-Jewish civilians are causally linked. A broader perspective of Nazi genocides is thus vital for a fuller understanding of the perpetration of the Holocaust. From this more holistic perspective, we can see how, for example, Auschwitz transformed from an execution site for Poles, initially, to an execution site, increasingly, for Soviet prisoners of war, to the largest death camp for Jews killed by the same method first pioneered on Soviet prisoners: Zyklon B gas.[117] Auschwitz was not the exception. Several other concentration camps holding substantial numbers of Jews and receiving Soviet prisoners early in the war experienced a burst of lethality.[118] The entwinement of Nazi policies of death is also apparent by the contemporaneous fate of starving Jews in ghettos, a routinely covered aspect of the Holocaust in documentaries, [119] and Soviet prisoners of war, whose fate is almost never covered. Yet the much more controlled environment of the prisoner-of-war camps made the Nazi-imposed starvation far more lethal there than in contemporary ghettos where Jews, despite facing execution if caught, could organize food on the black markets.[120] Nazi policies of death did not only target victim groups in similar ways. They also pinned them against each other. Increasingly short on labor as the German offensive halted, the Nazis adjusted their starvation policies towards Eastern Europeans in late 1941 to early 1942 and started selecting large numbers (about one million) of the terrorized and starved Soviet prisoners for forced labor that could involve digging mass graves for Jews shot by Nazi execution squads or guarding Jews awaiting their death in extermination camps. Thereby some of the Nazis’ victims became involved in the perpetration of the “Final Solution.”[121]

The Nazis did not manage to fulfill their plans of destruction. Nonetheless, they coerced over 10 million people to work, or enslaved them outright (the vast majority Eastern European and/or Jewish),[122] and up to 14 million non-combatants were killed by targeted Nazi policies.[123] Of these deaths, almost six million where Jews. It was on Jewish deaths the Nazis expended the most effort and their plans were most fully realized against Jews. The Nazi priorities of destruction are perhaps most apparent when considering that when the Nazis saw themselves forced to slow their annihilation of Soviet prisoners of war for practical reasons of labor recruitment in 1942, to do the same for the Jews was out of the question and the campaign to murder them was accelerated instead. Thus, the deaths of Soviet prisoners of war who had been by far the Germans’ largest victim group up to this point were overtaken by the deaths of Jews, as the anti-Jewish killing campaigns were expanded from execution commandos operating in the rear of the army to all Eastern European Jews, especially Poland, who were now to be murdered in newly erected extermination camps. Recruiting key staff from the earlier gassing campaigns against disabled people (among them Christian Wirth, of famed brutality, and Franz Stangl, commandant of Treblinka during its peak murderousness), the experimental extermination camp Chelmno (opened in December 1941) was joined by Sobibor (operational in April 1942), Belzec (operational in March 1942), and finally, largest of all, Treblinka (operational in July 1942).[124] In autumn 1942, these camps together with the kill commandos in the rear of the army, killed more people and at faster speeds than anyone in history.[125] Owing to their morbid success, the camps ran out of people to kill and, after inmate uprisings in Sobibor and Treblinka, were closed in late 1943. Jews from Western Europe, however, were only beginning to be deported and murdered, and Auschwitz, located at a Europe-wide rail hub (unlike the other extermination camps), was earmarked for the task. It began killing Jews in large numbers from summer 1942 on. When German frontlines collapsed and Nazi-allied Hungary shifted its allegiance late in the war, the Nazis forced a regime change and half a million Hungarian Jews, who had been largely protected by their government up to this point, were turned over to Auschwitz to be killed. Thus, although the war was long lost in 1944, Auschwitz’s gas chambers were frantically busy until Soviet liberation late in 1944. Nonetheless, on death marches to concentration camps in Germany tens of thousands more were to die from exhaustion, thirst, and malnutrition, and many died still even after Germany was totally defeated.

The postwar trials of Holocaust perpetrators and war criminals at the Nuremberg and successor trials were critical in establishing and prosecuting crimes against international law for the first time,[126] and the long-lasting demilitarization and peace in Europe as well as Japan’s official postwar pacifist stance (it underwent similar trials as Germany) seem to be at least in part attributable to the lasting impact of postwar justice.[127] At the same time, some of the findings are bewildering for today’s legal and moral standards, such as the shamelessly self-serving conclusion that international law permitted carpet and nuclear bombings of civilians.[128] Similarly, despite indubitable positive impacts of the trials on Holocaust and perpetrator historiography, some of the early legal omissions, for example that of the police, proved to have long-lasting impacts on omissions in historical memory and perpetrator research.[129]

Most of the top-level perpetrators of the Holocaust, if they were not dead or had managed to escape, were prosecuted at the Nuremberg trials. These included administrators (e.g. Hans Frank who governed the Polish territory where most extermination camps were located), paramilitary leaders (e.g. Ernst Kaltenbrunner who led the RSHA), and a small number of major industrialists (e.g. Gustav Krupp who was one of the heads of the large steel company involved in slave labor) as well as high-level military commanders (Karl Dönitz who headed the navy). Although in historiography the Einsatzgruppen shooting squads did not capture the same attention as the extermination camps for many decades, Einsatzgruppen leaders (such as Otto Ohlendorf, responsible for massacres of Jews in Ukraine and the Caucasus) were also prosecuted in a successor trial by the Americans. Nazi doctors (including Victor Brack who was one of the heads of T4), judges, and prosecutors also faced convictions by American-led trials. Further, particularly the Soviets, and countries newly-occupied by the Soviet Union in the postwar order, held national trials that also prosecuted Nazi officials slightly further down the food chain (e.g. Erich Koch, chief administrator of Ukraine, was condemned to death in Poland, and Friedrich Jeckeln, who headed one of the Einsatzgruppen, was condemned to death in Latvia). The international trials prosecuted 177 defendants, of whom 142 were convicted.[130] In all, there were tens of thousands of individual convictions across international and national-level trials; the justice dispatched varied greatly.[131]

Despite these efforts, many Nazi criminals escaped justice. Some prominently so by fleeing Europe only to be captured later, such as Adolf Eichmann, who was famously tried in Jerusalem in 1961, or Klaus Barbie, an infamous Gestapo officer and torturer, who was tried in 1987 in France. Others, like Auschwitz doctor Josef Mengele, escaped justice permanently. Yet, the majority of criminals who escaped justice managed to do so in less spectacular fashion, merely by falling below the radar or by a lack of evidence. Thus, notwithstanding the enormous energies expended by West Germany and, later, unified Germany on bringing perpetrators to court of the 140,000 individual defendants tried between the years 1945 and 2005, there were only 6,656 convictions as opposed to 5,184 acquittals. The staggering remainder of over 128,000 simply ended by the termination of their proceedings for lack of evidence or defendants being unfit for trial. Although another 9,459 sentences were passed in East Germany during its existence (1945-1989), the conclusion remains that the vast majority of perpetrators never saw the inside of a prison.[132] When we consider the broader scale of Nazi atrocities and genocides, particularly those committed by the Wehrmacht with a likely further 700,000 to 900,000 perpetrators,[133] only an approximate 2% perpetrators even ever received a trial verdict of any kind. A sobering statistic.

The Indonesian genocide

Indonesia in the 1960s was trying to walk the line between communism and Western capitalism. This was largely due to the leadership of president Sukarno, who had led Indonesia to independence from Dutch colonialization and was its first president thereafter. Nonetheless, Sukarno, like many post-colonial leaders, had perhaps more pronounced sympathies on the left, and Indonesia as a whole, like many other former colonies, had strong leftist movements. The communist party (PKI) with three million members as well as affiliated organizations, such as the feminist organization Gerwani, was the largest communist organization outside the Soviet Union and Mao’s China, and enjoyed a mass support base of 20 million (not least worker’s organizations and unions) which amounted to a quarter of Indonesia’s population of 100 million at the time.[134] As the Cold War was heating up, particularly in the region, Western powers led by the US had a keen interest in keeping Indonesia free of communism. Unlike the early communist movements in China and Russia (or Cambodia later), however, Indonesian communists were intent upon seeking power electorally, not militantly. A large and powerful organization, the Indonesian military, courted by the US,[135] itself exhibited fissions between more left-leaning and decidedly authoritarian wings.[136]

It was a left-leaning group within the army, together with a small handful of Indonesian Communist Party leaders, including its head Dipa Aidit, that attempted a military coup on September 30, 1965, proclaiming to be protecting president Sukarno from a rightwing military conspiracy. [137] They captured and killed seven army generals, but were soon thwarted by a rightwing countercoup led by general Suharto which equally claimed to be protecting president Sukarno and had been preparing, in close cooperation with the US, for such an opportunity to eliminate communists and oust president Sukarno who otherwise enjoyed too much popular support for any military-led direct move against him to be successful.[138] Blaming the entire communist party and its many affiliate organizations, the main political rival, Suharto orchestrated a mass killing and mass incarceration campaign that swept across the country’s numerous islands. Western countries adopted a stance of at times quiet acquiescence, loud praise, or even provided, not least in the case of the US, active support by, among others, compiling communist party membership lists, funding anticommunist actions, and lending “psywar” expertise for propaganda purposes.[139] Conservative estimates suggest at least 500,000 were killed, and further 1.5 million were detained in prisons and camps only to be released by 1979 after 13 years without ever facing trials or charges but now confronted with broken families and a lifetime of discrimination.[140]

The 1965 countercoup proved to be completely transformative for Indonesia. It enabled Suharto to wrest power from Sukarno over the next two years and the once immensely popular president would die under house arrest in 1970. Suharto now in power would remain there for over three decades until he was ousted in 1998 owing to his corruption. Communists, leftist movements, and even unions to this day, have to move with considerable caution in Indonesia because of strong prevailing anti-communist sentiments. Suharto would commit, arguably, at least one more in occupied East Timor (1975-1999), where a quarter of the population, or around 150,000, died during the first few years of the conquest,[141] and commit atrocities as well as massive human rights violations bordering on genocide in West Papua which Indonesia occupied (although some of the conflict preceded Suharto), despite Dutch attempts to hand independence to its former colony.[142]

Given how transformative it was for the fate of Indonesia, however, surprisingly much about the anti-communist mass murder remains unknown. The vast archipelago that is Indonesia with its many different cultures, languages, religions, and massive geographic spread adds considerably to the challenge. Geoffrey Robinson, among the senior researchers of mass violence under Suharto, notes that

there were significant variations in the pattern of the killing. Geographically, they were most concentrated in the populous provinces of Central and East Java, on the islands of Bali, in Aceh and North Sumatra, and in parts of East Nusa Tenggara. By contrast, they were relatively limited in the capital city of Jakarta, the province of West Java, and much of Sulawesi and Maluku. The timing of the killing was also distinctive. It began in Aceh in early October, and spread to Central Java in late October and to East Java and North Sumatra in early November. In December 1965, a full two months after the alleged coup attempt, the violence finally started in Bali, where an estimated eighty thousand people were killed in a few months. Meanwhile, on the largely Catholic island of Flores toward the eastern end of the archipelago, it did not begin until February of the following year. The violence started to slow significantly in March 1966, shortly after the army seized power, but continued intermittently in some parts of the country through 1968.[143]

What we do know is that the army even where it did not directly participate in the killings was crucial to organizing the mass murder whether by providing lists, lending vehicles and materiel, or securing murder sites.[144] It is also clear that, similarly to the Armenian and Rwandan genocide, the violence involved a remarkably high degree of civilian participation, particularly from members of religious or political youth organizations, [145] and as in those genocides the main method of killing in Indonesia was with edged or blunt weapons or tools, not guns. On the island of Sumatra, the perhaps most prominent perpetrator group was the paramilitary Pemuda Pancasila (Pancasila Youth), founded by a general (A. H. Nasution) to promote nationalist and military interests, but independent of any political affiliation or even the army.[146] In East and Central Java, however, among the most fervent participants in mass murder were members of Ansor and Bansor, youth groups of the influential Islamic organization Nahdlatul Ulama (NU),[147] but also the youth group (Pemuda Marhaen) of the communist party’s main political rival, the Indonesian Nationalist Party (PNI), which in turn played the most prominent role on the island of Bali.[148] In the Catholic dominated island Flores (the largest island of the East Nusa Tenggara province), in turn, it was the Catholic youth of the Catholic party (Pemuda Katolik) that participated with the strong encouragement of its religious leaders who staunchly supported the military’s anticommunist agenda.[149] Nonetheless, the centrality of the military in initiating, organizing, escalating, but also stopping the violence is perhaps most notable in the case of Aceh, where an army office initiated and coordinated the extermination campaign carried out by the civilian groups whereas heretofore the violence had remained confined to pogroms,[150] and the case of Bali where mass killings only began after a special paracommando of the army (RPKAD) arrived and left-leaning local military and political leaders removed.[151] In West Java, on the other hand, the local commander, like his counterpart in Aceh, was opposed to the communist party and had loyal troops at hand but decided to on mass arrests rather than killing.[152] The military was also responsible for setting up the umbrella organization (Komando Aksi Pengganyangan Gerakan September Tiga Puluh, better known as KAP-Gestapu) which coordinated the different civilian groups in their killings.[153] Again, the army was also central in containing and stopping the killing once the objective of breaking communist power had been achieved, as the scholar Jess Melvin has documented in Aceh.[154]

The alliance of the different groups suggests an underlying convergence in their anticommunist goals, yet also a substantial divergence in their particular reasons for participating. Participation of religious groups was likely primarily motivated by the purported or actual anti-religious character of communists.[155] Nationalist groups, in turn, competed with communists over political power. Yet again, these differing motivations are not neatly separable, given for example that the parent-organizations of the religious youth groups often were themselves or at least sponsored political parties which competed in elections (e.g. the name of parent organization of the Pemuda Katolik is tellingly the Partai Katolik – the Catholic Party). There were also socio-economic reasons for participating in violence that could cut across but also differentiate by political affiliation. Several regions with intense killings, such as Central and East Java as well as Bali, had been experiencing considerable tensions over land reforms seeking to redistribute land to landless peasants and over communist-affiliated unions’ demanding of better conditions for workers.[156] But ethnic targeting also occurred in the killings of Chinese or Indonesian Chinese, who in the eyes of the perpetrators combined the undesirable traits of being foreign, wealthy (often being merchants or shopkeepers), and supposedly connected to communist China.[157] Although men were disproportionately slated for extermination (as is common during genocide), women experienced widespread sexual violence and torture during mass detainment (also common in genocide),[158] while perpetrators were overwhelmingly men, as in all other cases of genocide. The genderedness of this genocide is also particularly apparent by the fact that a key target in persecution but also in government propaganda was the feminist organization Gerwani (Gerakan Wanita Indonesia – Indonesian Women’s Movement) that was affiliated with the communists. Perpetrator groups were typically highly patriarchal and felt threatened by women’s rights. Government propaganda during and after the mass killings played into these sexual politics by claiming (falsely) the Gerwani had sexually tortured, emasculated, and eventually killed the seven generals that died in the coup while performing a sexually perverted quasi orgy.[159]

The government propaganda was not only successful at urging participation in anticommunist violence, but also had a considerable afterlife extending beyond even the thirty-year reign of the Suharto regime itself. Perpetrators of genocidal violence under Suharto remain without punishment, [160] anticommunist and anti-leftist sentiments persist to this day, and in some cases leading perpetrators even boast openly (including on national television and, famously, in documentaries) about their deeds.[161] This degree of impunity in Indonesia is arguably comparable only to genocides before the concept of genocide existed: the Herero and Nama genocide and the Armenian genocide—although even in the latter case at least some perpetrator trials, however fraught and brief, were held as we have seen.

The Cambodian genocide

When in the 1960s a Cambodian communist group called the Khmer Rouge started an insurgency against the government and the king Norodom Sihanouk, their success at taking the entire country seemed implausible. The Khmer Rouge had its origins in the larger communist movement led by Ho Chi Minh that spanned Cambodia, Vietnam, and Laos, which then constituted the French colony of Indochina, in reaction to French colonialism. Its leaders were privileged upper middle class who became communist party members while undertaking their studies in France. After many years in hiding and forming an insurgency base in a remote part of Cambodia, the communist uprising gained a substantial influx of willing fighters by the American carpet bombing of the Cambodian countryside (1965-1973, most intensely 1970-1973) to drive out Vietnamese forces that routinely tactically withdrew to inaccessible parts of Cambodia while fighting the Americans in the Vietnam war. Large numbers of peasants who had lost cattle, farms, and family during the American bombings flocked to the Khmer Rouge. Further momentum was inadvertently provided after the American-supported authoritarian coup by Cambodian general Lon Nol (1970) who unseated the popular king Sihanouk. Sihanouk, in turn, threw his weight behind the Khmer Rouge who now endorsed him and benefited from Sihanouk’s broad rural base—a key target group for the communists. Lon Nol’s harsh repression was unsuccessful at containing the uprising and only aggravated widespread grievances, as the Americans increasingly drew down troops in Vietnam and consequently took only a waning interest in the wider region. With the support of North Vietnam, the Khmer Rouge fully defeated Lon Nol when taking the capital Phnom Penh in 1975.[162]

What ensued was a reorganization of society that, arguably, is without parallel in modern history. Schools were effectively abolished, money and nearly all forms of private property ceased to exist, urban populations were distributed across the countryside, families were increasingly broken up and collective eating and farming was established region by region.[163] That such a complete social overhaul could only be achieved with extreme measures, repression, and violence, is ultimately unsurprising. The Cambodian genocide would cost the lives of 1.6 to 2.25 million people from violence, overwork, and starvation amounting to between a fifth and almost a quarter of the total population. The death toll from violence alone was 728,000 to 1.455 million, over 80% of whom were men.[164] Among the genocides covered here, the Cambodian genocide stands out for its having been committed by an explicitly communist government. The Khmer Rouge communist utopian ideology hoping to fundamentally reshape politics, industry, society, and family together with the large number of deaths from starvation and overwork shares some similarities with other catastrophic attempts at transforming countries in the image of communism in Mao’s Great Leap China (1958-1962) that produced a famine killing up to 45 million,[165] and the mass starvation during Stalin’s industrialization campaigns from 1932 to 1933 that may have killed between five and eight million, raging particularly severely in Ukraine and Kazakhstan.[166] Insofar as the Khmer Rouge were supported by communist China and the policies of Mao’s Great Leap and the Cultural Revolution were a source of inspiration, albeit in varied forms, these associations are not entirely misleading.[167] Gross negligence, economic incompetence, mistrust of experts paired with incorrigible ideological blindness of Khmer Rouge leaders, as evinced in the 1976 Four Year Plan,[168] greatly contributed to the general state of deprivation of Cambodians and many deaths from starvation. Coinciding with the new Four Year Plan, persecutions and political arrests skyrocketed to push through the policies and assert control.[169]

Although virtually no Cambodian was unaffected by the radical policies of the Khmer Rouge, the large aggregate numbers occlude great regional variations as well as variations in different ethnic and economic groups from the policies. At the beginning of their rise to power, the Khmer Rouge had removed people from urban population centres, most of whom were in fact famished rural refugees fleeing the civil war between the government and the Khmer Rouge,[170] and distributed them in chaotic fashion across the countryside of different regions. The North-West zone, for example, received a particularly large influx of these urban dwellers, now classified as second-class citizens and called new people, but lacked foodstuffs to nourish the newcomers. Within a year up to 40% of the new arrivals were dead from starvation.[171] Furthermore, compared to either Mao or Stalin, the proportion of violent killing to deaths by starvation under the Khmer Rouge is far higher and the especially lethal targeting of ethnic minorities are more particular to the Khmer Rouge (although such persecution existed under both Stalin and Mao).[172] All foreign languages were banned as were any minority ethno-cultural practices; religion was abolished in any case.[173] Among the minorities, the Vietnamese made the most routine appearances in party speeches and manifestos as the enemy and were the most lethally persecuted. Right at Pol Pot’s takeover of power, about half of the Vietnamese-Cambodians, victims of historic anti-Vietnamese enmities and Pol Pot’s particularly rabid nationalist communism, were expelled to Vietnam; of the remaining population virtually everyone was dead by the time the Khmer Rouge were unseated from power by the invading Vietnamese army.[174] Similarly, the Muslim Cham minority in the country faced double the death rate (more than 36%) compared to ethnic Khmer Cambodians (18.7%).[175] In other cases, however, what appears to have been disproportionate targeting based on ethnic markers reveals itself to be driven by class and political ideology. 50% of ethnic Chinese died, among the highest proportions of death of any group, largely owing, it seems, to the fact that Cambodian Chinese were perceived to be urbanites and traders, and thus they were collectively treated as class enemies.[176]

The temporal trajectory of the deaths is also highly revealing of the causes of perpetration. The first most systematic lethal target of the Khmer Rouge were those who posed the greatest threat to their power: employees and soldiers of the previous Lon Nol regime. [177] What ensued was a struggle to centralize control over what during the civil war had often been relatively independent armed groups led by Khmer Rouge luminaries who delegated large patronage networks consisting of family members and other forms of allegiances. Among the first efforts at control was the monopolization of military violence by forming a central army in 1975.[178] Nonetheless, large regional patronage networks persisted which in turn meant that purges in an effort of seizing power would mean the eradication of the entire network, particularly of any family relations that, in Cambodian tradition, constituted its core.[179] Regional uprisings, non-compliance, or at least perceived non-compliance, with central policies led to widespread purges across the country.[180] The purges were particularly severe in the North-West Zone in 1977,[181] and most severe during preparations for war with Vietnam in the Vietnam-bordering Eastern Zone from 1977 onwards, both of which were led by South-West cadres.[182] In either case, mass killings accompanied harshly restrictive and punitive food and labor policies that led to mass starvations.

More than Auschwitz for the Holocaust, and arguably more problematically so, the torture prison S-21 has become the symbol of Khmer Rouge persecution. Located in the capital Phnom Penh, the heart of the Central Zone, it served predominantly as the interrogation and torture site for purged high-ranking Khmer Rouge cadres before they were ferried off to be killed—for with the exception of seven known survivors, everyone of the around 14,000 known (and likely many more unknown) inmates at S-21 was killed—in the killing fields of Choeung Ek in the outskirts of the city.[183] Due to being intricately linked to central party politics, the political prominence of many of its victims, and due to being one of the few sites of murder where the Khmer Rouge left written and visual documents, S-21 is of outsized historical importance which, undoubtedly, has also contributed to its symbolic importance in post-genocide memory. Nonetheless, likely the majority of prisoners were themselves members of the broad Khmer Rouge political, security, or military apparatus, which is why most prisoners were “young, ethic Khmer males from rural backgrounds” indistinguishable from the people who guarded and interrogated them.[184] Whereas the class-based enemies who constitute most of Khmer Rouge victims in absolute numbers were instead held and killed in regional camps or simply in the fields.[185] About 10% of S-21 prisoners whose “confessions”, coerced during the torturous interrogation process, were among the 500 that have been preserved “had held positions of responsibility” within the Khmer Rouge organization and thirty had once even belonged to the Central Party Committee.[186] The widespread paranoia of Khmer Rouge leaders and infighting in the wider organization also meant that few of the approximately 500,000 total cadres were beyond suspicion,[187] and thus the surprisingly substantial portion of at least 79 S-21 guards ended up in S-21 themselves.[188] The proportion of cadres purged—and unlike in Mao’s China and the Soviet Union such purges were almost exclusively lethal—stood at 25%,[189] a number significantly outstripping the 18.7% of the perished civilian population estimated by the demographic experts of the International Tribunal prosecuting the perpetrators (ECCC) and that is only eclipsed by the proportions of murdered minorities under Pol Pot.[190] S-21 is hence ultimately most representative of a particular form of Khmer Rouge persecution directed against its own members, rather than victimhood under the Khmer Rouge more generally. S-21 is emblematic of a regime that eventually implicated all of the highest-ranking cadre but Pol Pot and his second-hand man Nuon Chea of collusion with the enemy, leading even to Pol Pot’s military strong-man Ta Mok to be eventually put under house arrest and the former head of S-21 and of security Son Sen and his family murdered.[191]

The Khmer Rouge sealed their own fate when, beginning in 1977, they launched attacks against their bigger and militarily far more sophisticated neighbour Vietnam. In fast pincer movements down Cambodia’s infrastructural arteries, the Vietnamese deposed of the Khmer Rouge within a mere two weeks in January 1979. The Khmer Rouge leaders fled to the Thai border where they regrouped and for nearly two more decades would launch guerilla attacks supported by a motley of allies comprising the US, China—both seeking to counter the Vietnamese-Soviet might in the region—and ASEAN. The Cambodian-Vietnamese administration then running the country almost immediately held a trial of Khmer Rouge leaders Pol Pot and Ieng Sary that judged them guilty of genocide.[192] International trials of Khmer Rouge leaders, however, would only begin in the 2000s and were confined to prosecuting a mere handful of surviving top leaders (Pol Pot had died in 1998). The court (known as the ECCC) issued three guilty verdicts against Duch (head of S-21), Nuon Chea, and Khieu Samphan (chief ideologue of the Khmer Rouge), including, for the latter two, genocide judgments for the mass murder of Cham and Cambodian-Vietnamese. [193]

The Rwandan genocide

Before colonization Rwanda was a kingdom ruled by a Tutsi king and a Tutsi-ethnic quasi-aristocracy of pastoralists. The majority people in this kingdom, however, were ethnic Hutu, and there was a small minority of ethnic Twa (belonging to the pygmy peoples). The Rwandan kingdom expanded over centuries until German colonizers appropriated the lands during the “scramble for Africa” in the late 19th century. The Germans exploited the efficiently organized structure of the kingdom to govern the colony with few institutional changes. During WWI the Belgians acquired the German colony, but likewise utilized the ruling Tutsi to govern the country to minimize the need for Belgian resources or manpower. Although the socio-ethnic categories of Hutu and Tutsi had already been relatively immutable prior to colonization,[194] German and Belgian colonizers imported social Darwinist ideas of race which rigidified the divisions between the supposedly superior and more Caucasian race of the Tutsi and the “slave” races of Hutus and Twa and providing further justification for the preservation of rigid colonial hierarchies.[195] The Belgian-introduced identity cards on which the “race” was recorded would provide a critical means for perpetrators to identify Tutsis during the genocide.[196] Despite this, colonial rule in Rwanda, as well as its “twin” neighbor Burundi with its almost identical ethnic makeup, was marked rather by territorial and institutional continuities than discontinuities.[197]

In the aftermath of WWII as much of the colonized world inched or sped towards decolonization, much of the thrust among Hutus to free themselves of Belgian colonizers also was directed against the Tutsi who were perceived, rightly and, in the case of many Tutsi peasants, wrongly, to be oppressors. Hence, in 1957 a group of Hutu intellectuals, including independent Rwanda’s first president Grégoire Kayibanda, drafted the Bahutu Manifesto denouncing Tutsi oppression against Tutsis,[198] and increasing inter-ethnic tensions led to anti-Tutsi pogroms and violence in the street which left 919 Tutsi and 312 Hutu dead in 1959.[199] The Belgians seeking to prepare Rwanda for independence attempted to establish a democracy that now meant the Hutu majority would exercise far more political power than heretofore. The Tutsi elite only stood to lose its hold on power in the process and was thus more resistant to changes, whereas the Hutu elite became the drivers of independence. The Hutu revolution, at once a postcolonial independence movement as well as pro-Hutu ideology, eventually proclaimed independence and Kayibanda became president. Independence in 1962 was accompanied with the establishment of a discriminatory anti-Tutsi regime and more violence that was predominantly suffered by Tutsis. The violence and discrimination meant a continual exodus of Tutsi refugees into neighboring countries, Zaïre (today’s DRC), Uganda, Tanzania, and especially Tutsi-governed Burundi, so that by 1964 there were already 336,000 Tutsi refugees—a number which by the 1990s had grown to a total of between 600,000 and 700,000.[200]

Over the next decades Tutsi refugees would regularly launch incursions into Rwanda, largely with little military success, and within Rwanda’s borders Hutus, spurred on by the president’s anti-Tutsi demagoguery, would launch assaults against Tutsi civilians. Such patterns would recur during the 1990s genocide. One such massacre taking place after a 1963 refugee invasion would cost 10,000 Tutsi lives.[201] In neighboring Burundi, in turn, news of such massacres of Tutsis hardened the governing Tutsi elite and radicalized Tutsi refugees so that, in response to a 1972 Hutu insurrection, the Tutsi army together with civilian groups exterminated between 200,000 and 300,000 Hutu civil servants, intellectuals, and political leaders.[202] In a feedback process of violence, Hutus fled to Rwanda bearing news of Tutsi atrocities, providing further anti-Tutsi propaganda value for the faltering dictator Kayibanda, and when in 1994 the opportunity arose to take revenge or to secure against Tutsi atrocities by murdering all Rwandan Tutsis many of these Hutu refugees took it.[203]

A decade of blatantly anti-Tutsi rule in Rwanda was followed, in 1973, by Juvenal Habyarimana’s coup d’état which ousted Kayibanda and established a more moderate, albeit similarly authoritarian, regime that managed to grow education, stabilize the economy, improve public health and welfare as well as reduce inter-ethnic violence.[204] Yet, in the mid-1980s falling coffee and tin prices, two main Rwandan exports, spelled economic trouble, and owing to destitution, hunger, disease, and increasing levels of inter-personal violence from 1984 on, adult life expectancy dropped abysmally.[205] By the late 1980s, Habyarimana’s previously shameless espousal of single-party rule had mellowed enough, not least because of eroding popular support in the wake of crisis, to allow for the introduction of multi-party politics.[206] This offered more political space to Tutsis in the country and refugees abroad—but also Hutu supremacist hardliners, not least within Habyarimana’s own party which now had to concede power to others.[207] Rifts within Habyarimana’s governing party also began forming. These were enormously aggravated after Habyarimana’s assassination when it split into factions over whether the country’s Tutsi population could be trusted.[208]

Concomitantly, across Rwanda’s northern border in Uganda of the 1980s, a Tutsi refugee group, the Rwandan Patriotic Front (RPF), had joined Yoweri Museveni’s forces and succeeded in ousting the unpopular Ugandan president Milton Obote to install Museveni at the helm of power in 1986.[209] The battle-hardened RPF, which was a foreign element in Uganda despite close ties to Museveni and was confronted with local animosity in Uganda, renewed its attention to invading Rwanda in the late 1980s where the inauguration of multi-party politics and negotiations for refugees to return between Rwanda and Uganda seemed to threaten the legitimacy of the militarized RPF claiming to be fighting a totalitarian dictatorship that would never let the refugees return.[210]

The RPF thus invaded Rwanda in 1990. After many years of respite, the previous patterns of Tutsi refugee invasion followed by Hutus massacring Rwandan Tutsis reemerged, although initially the massacres remained at limited scale (some hundreds or low thousands) between 1990 and the onset of genocide in 1994.[211] From 1990 until 1993 the civil war was relatively uneventful and largely presented itself as a stalemate where, nonetheless, the military balance increasingly favored the RPF. Peace negotiations brokered by international actors were protracted and concluded, temporarily, in the summer of 1993 with a deal that highly favored the RPF, stipulating, for example, that the RPF contribute 50% of officers in the Rwandan army and 40% of rank-and-file.[212] The peace agreement angered hardliners in the Rwandan government as well as the armed forces, half of whom were now threated by demobilization in times of serious economic peril.[213]

In autumn of 1993, they would be provided with further fuel. Burundi’s first democratically elected Hutu president, the moderate Melchior Ndadaye, was assassinated only months after the elections by members of the Tutsi-dominated army in an abortive coup attempt. The murder of the first high-profile Hutu politician in Rwanda’s “twin” Burundi where political and executive power had remained in Tutsi hand effectively demonstrated to Hutu hardliners in Hutu-dominated Rwanda that only with militant power concentrated in Hutu hands could Hutus be protected. The ensuing internecine killings of Hutus and Tutsis in Burundi meant that between 200,000 and 350,000 Hutu refugees streamed into Rwanda providing further credibility to stories of Tutsi murderousness and, during the genocide in 1994, would lend a helping hand in the mass murder of Tutsi civilians.[214] Sporadic killings of Tutsis but also of some pro-Hutu militias (Interahamwe) not deemed sufficiently reliable and extremists began in Rwanda.[215]

The atmosphere was then already charged when the plane by president Habyarimana was shot down.[216] Habyarimana’s death provided the window of opportunity. Hardliners immediately blamed the RPF and called for the elimination of all Tutsis. Théoneste Bagosora, a Hutu extremist who, among others, commanded the Presidential Guard, the most elite unit in the Rwandan forces, immediately set upon ordering his forces to murder political moderates, including then moderate prime minister Agathe Uwilingiyimana, her family, and her Belgian peacekeeper guards, and Tutsis in the capital.[217] A power struggle between moderates and extremists within the ruling party and, particularly, between the ruling party and the opposition ensued that lasted for two weeks (in some provinces three) with typical patterns being the replacement, and often killing of, moderates with hardliners who in turn implemented the gathering and destruction of Tutsis in the area. Regions with the least Hutu-Tutsi intermarriage, small numbers of Tutsis, where the governing party polled particularly strongly, or where RPF incursion loomed as an immediate threat experienced particularly speedy onset and comprehensive violence. [218] Yet, in certain cases violence began before extremists captured regional centers of power, suggesting that there was considerable popular and local support in the commission of mass violence or that individual “entrepreneurs” of violence, drawing on existing institutional structures, not least militarized youth wings of political parties, or social networks, initiated massacres of their own accord.[219] The RPF immediately recommenced its invasion from the north while a contingent of 600 elite RPF troops stationed in the capital as part of the peace deal held out against attacks by the Presidential Guard and affiliated units and slowly gained the upper hand. Regions (in the north) already held by or quickly falling to the invading RPF experienced no or only very limited genocidal violence.[220] The RPF operated with considerable efficiency and sophisticated tactics in conquering the country. Nonetheless, the sheer speed of the genocide, lack of international intervention, and the fact that the RPF optimized its campaign for military victory rather than the saving of Tutsi lives meant that hundreds of thousands died.[221]

Meanwhile the international community did its best to avoid intervention and tiptoed around using the word “genocide” in public communications. Nations with embassies and expat populations in the country pulled them out within the first days of the violence onset in the capital. Save for a very small number of reporters and camera operators, international media similarly deserted the country. Hence, most images of the genocide are either taken in the immediate aftermath, once the RPF had conquered the country, or during the international exodus at the beginning of the genocide. Most of the images of the genocide were thus focused on the roadblocks in the capital Kigali. The few international peacekeepers stationed in Rwanda to assure the Arusha peace agreement was enforced were largely powerless to stop the massacres, and indeed were drawn down when several Belgian peacekeepers were killed at the beginning of the genocide. Nonetheless, they likely managed to save tens of thousands.[222] The only international power more heavily involved, with problematic motivations and outcomes, was France. Having lost its colonies, desperate to retain influence in Francophone Africa, and having nurtured a relatively close relationship with the reigning Hutu regime, France sent troops with humanitarian objectives (Opération Turquoise), but its underlying motivations were undoubtedly more selfish and the genocidal perpetrators in any case welcomed French troops thinking they had come to repel the RPF, as French troops had done in previous years since the start of the civil war in 1990. This operation may have saved some from massacres, but the protective mantle of the French troops meant that many of the perpetrators fled to neighboring Zaïre (today’s DRC) taking a large part of Rwanda’s Hutu population with them.[223]

The typical pattern of murdering was to erect roadblocks and checking all passersby for their ID. Tutsis would often be killed on the spot, predominantly with edged weapons or tools. Perpetrators “were average adult Hutu men—in terms of age, education, paternity, and occupation,” which meant that most of them had several years of primary education, were farmers, their dominant age range was 15-44, and most had at least one child.[224] The only demographic marker that is a clear predictor of perpetratorhood was the perpetrators’ gender—they were overwhelmingly men.[225] Killings were often led by civilian authorities and administrators, although armed soldiers or gendarmes were also often present, who rallied groups of perpetrators in an historical, institutional context where rallying men for mandatory government labor (Umuganda) was common practice.[226] This meant that killing happened almost exclusively in groups, [227] and that participation in mass murder is strongly predicted by the number of other participants in the neighborhood and whether one had any kinship or other ties to participants.[228] It is thus perhaps not surprising that perpetrators most commonly give positive and negative social pressures (e.g. critical mass of participants in a given area or negative physical or social repercussions for non-participation) as motivations for participation in violence, followed by obedience to authority, civil war-related fears of Tutsis, and the desire to avenge Habyarimana’s death (blamed on Tutsis), but also the possibility to materially profit from looting victim property.[229] Another characteristic of the murdering in groups was that around 70% of the group members seem not to have directly killed or killed a small number of victims, whereas a small minority of extremely prolific murderers was primarily responsibility for the death toll.[230] Most of the perpetrators were in some form affiliated with the ruling party (Habyarimana’s MRND) or, although far fewer, the largest and more moderate opposition MDR party.[231] Only a minority (2.5%) were part of the most extreme anti-Tutsi party, the CDR, or its Impuzamugambi militia.[232] Many groups were substantially composed of civilians and were formed ad hoc, nonetheless, organized armed perpetrator groups like the Interahamwe militia—the ruling party’s youth wing that with the onset of civil war had increasingly been geared towards what was claimed to be armed defense—soldiers, and gendarmes were important drivers of the genocide in terms of initiating violence, breaking the most persistent Tutsi resistance, for recruiting civilians for participation, and ensuring that participants in the violence did not break ranks.

Notwithstanding the extraordinary importance of organized armed groups during events of mass violence, the Rwandan genocide, similar to the Armenian, Indonesian, and likely the Cambodian genocides, exhibited a high degree of participatory violence relative to more formally organized, armed groups. Less like the cases of the Armenian and Indonesian genocides which were committed by societies with large populations (Ottoman-Turkey and Indonesia), but likely very similar to the Cambodian and, relative to the German settler population, the Herero and Nama genocides, the proportion of perpetrators to the population in the Rwandan genocide is significant. Remarkably, unlike all of these genocides, we have quite detailed numbers in the Rwandan case, because we have detailed census data from 1991 before the genocide, because the genocide was recent, and because an enormously large number of perpetrators was prosecuted. Thus, the number of perpetrators directly involved in killing ranges from about 175,000 to 423,000 or about 14% to 23% percent of the active adult male Hutu population.[233] Of course, this range is large. Yet, we can believe them with more confidence and they are more detailed than estimates from most other genocides. The proportion of perpetrators is staggering and contrasts with that of Nazi perpetrators relative to the German population which stood at perhaps about 2-3% of the adult male population.[234] Another characteristic that the Rwandan genocide likely shares with the Armenian, Indonesian, and Cambodian genocides is that probably a majority of victims was killed by edged weapons or tools which in turn entails that the ratio of perpetrators to victims is very high. Taking 230,000 perpetrators to 600,000 victims we arrive at a ratio of well over 1/3 as opposed to about 1/10 for victims of Nazi atrocities when calculating with a total of 12 million non-combatant Nazi victims and 1.1 million perpetrators of mass killings. We will revisit the enormous obstacles these proportions pose when discussing post-genocide trials further below.

The genocide was ended when the RPF fully conquered the country. In a hundred days the majority of the country’s Tutsi population had been murdered. Had the perpetrators not been stopped, the overwhelming evidence suggests that the extermination of Tutsi would have been total. The most commonly given death toll of Tutsi victims of the Rwandan genocide is 800,000, and the current (RPF-dominated) Rwandan government claims a victim toll of 1,074,017 of whom 93.7% it believes are Tutsi.[235] However, these numbers, certainly those of the Rwandan government, are flawed and lump in victims from the civil war and post-genocide reprisals. Contemporary sophisticated analyses with a variety of methods and data suggest a Tutsi victim toll that ranges between 491,000 and 522,000 (a proportion of between 61% and 65% of all Rwandan Tutsi)[236] or also between 562,000 and 662,000 Tutsi (69% to 81.5%).[237] Rape of Tutsi women (and some men) was ubiquitous, possibly running in the hundreds of thousands.[238] The death toll of Hutus is far more uncertain and the causes of Hutu deaths are far more divergent, ranging from direct violence (itself with a wide range of motivations and causes) to malnutrition and disease. Hutu moderates, killed as part of the genocide, seem to have died in the tens of thousands (perhaps around 25,000 to 30,000),[239] and a conservative estimate of Hutus killed in RPF reprisals in the immediate aftermath of the genocide is typically given as 25,000 to 45,000,[240] but some estimates have an upper range of 100,000.[241] Exhausted and malnourished Hutu refugees, among them perpetrators but overwhelmingly women and children, fled to camps in Zaïre and were soon visited upon by one of the most lethal outbreaks of Cholera ever measured with 50,000 deaths in the space of a few weeks.[242] In reaction to militant refugee incursions into Rwanda, the RPF-led Rwandan government invaded Zaïre (soon to be renamed Democratic Republic of the Congo) to dissolve the camps either by refugee repatriation or by massacre. Constant flight, mass killings by Rwandan and allied Zairean rebel forces, malnutrition, and further disease outbreaks—reinforced by the frequently practiced denial and destruction of humanitarian aid by Rwandan and allied forces—likely cost the lives of between 200,000 and 233,000 Hutu refugees.[243] In total, between 1994 and 1997 there were probably around 542,000 Hutu deaths, albeit with an uncertainty band of between 212,000 and 1.3 million, from the war, mass murder during the genocide, from RPF reprisals, or from disease during war.[244] The most unknown and least researched victim group of the genocide are the ethnic Twa (or Batwa). They belong to the pygmy ethnicity, live semi-nomadically in forested regions,[245] and constituted about 0.4% of the Rwandan pre-genocide population.[246] Occupying the bottom of the colonial racial hierarchy,[247] yet posing no political threat or military threat as Rwanda’s Tutsi seemingly did when the Tutsi-dominated RPF invaded, the Twa were not as deliberately targeted as the Tutsi during the genocide, but their “race” played a major role in their persecution.[248] Their genocidal death toll is commonly given as 10,000, [249] about a third of their 28,000 population in 1991. [250]

With perhaps up to 2.1 million displaced persons and over a million total deaths out of a population of likely less than 8 million at the onset of the war and genocide, post-genocide Rwanda was facing enormous obstacles.[251] The enormous number of perpetrators in a now Tutsi dominated government posed enormous questions as to justice, reconciliation, and integration. Furthermore, many perpetrators, along with their families, had fled across the border and were launching incursions into Rwanda hoping to unseat the government. Rwanda’s answer was twofold. To combat its external enemies, it dissolved the Congolese camps by force, which in turn set the stage for subsequent conflict in Congo. Several wars that involved nine African states would be fought on Congolese territory between 1996 and 2003 ensuing a plausible death toll of between 550,000 to 2.4 million (although a more widely circulated but likely flawed study estimated between 3.9 and 5.4 million deaths), mostly from causes indirectly related to the violence (deprivation, starvation, exposure to disease).[252] Rwanda has remained the most persistent overt or covert external agent involved in violent conflict in Congo over the last decades.[253]

To deal with the genocidal perpetrators who had remained in the country or had been caught, Rwanda embarked on an unprecedented judicial experiment. Initially, perpetrators were incarcerated en masse. Many would die from overcrowding and lack of hygiene.[254] Slowly, however, prison conditions improved and, given an overwhelmed justice system with few trained judges, the government created village-level grass roots courts, called Gacaca, where testimonies of survivors, family members, and bystanders would be heard and the accused would be judged by local judges elected by their community. In this way, 2 million cases and 1 million suspects—effectively the majority of the adult male Hutu population at the time of genocide—were prosecuted.[255] Punishments ranged from further imprisonment (by the time of the Gacaca most suspects had been imprisoned for a decade), community work like building houses for survivors, and full release. The most serious cases nonetheless were still prosecuted by more formal organs in Rwanda or, in the case of the major architects of the genocide, the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda, located in Arusha, Tanzania, where over the next two decades 93 suspects were indicted and 82 perpetrators convicted.[256] With its maximal prosecution, Rwanda’s judicial approach to perpetrators thus sits at one end of the spectrum at whose other end is the non-prosecution of perpetrators of the Indonesian mass killings or German colonial atrocities, and where the prosecution of Holocaust or perpetrators of the Bosnian genocide and war sit somewhere in the middle. Although on the surface, such total prosecution of perpetrators would seem to be the fullest delivery of justice, questions remain over the Rwandan state’s near blanket treatment of adult male Hutus as perpetrators while no prosecution of RPF crimes took place at the gacaca and the Rwandan government actively interfered with any investigation into RPF crimes at the international tribunal.[257]

The Bosnian genocide

The first incarnation of a Yugoslav state under whose banner Slovenes, Croats, Serbs, but also substantial numbers of Albanians and a variety of multi-ethnic Muslim populations who had flourished there under Ottoman rule, came into being in the aftermath of WWI. Ruled as a kingdom until WWII, tensions were mounting in the 1930s owing to the political ambitions and territorial claims of fascist Germany and Italy as well as the communist Soviet Union, nationalist tensions within the different territories of Yugoslavia, and the political ideologies of fascism versus communism which replicated in various forms within Yugoslavia itself. Eventually, in 1941, Axis powers (Italy, Bulgaria, Germany) invaded Yugoslavia and split its territories between them, but with the German invasion of the Soviet Union the territory would experience a veritable civil war in which the Croatian fascist Ustaše would fight royalist Serbian Četniks and the multi-ethnic communist Partisans led by Josip Broz Tito. Although all sides fought with merciless vehemence leading to the death of about a million Yugoslavs, it was the Ustaše who would outdo the other groups with genocidal violence, often incredibly brutal, committed against particularly Serbs, Romani, and Jews, but also Muslims and political opponents, with Serbs constituting the majority of its 300,000 or more victims.[258] Tito’s partisan movement emerged successful from WWII and led to the refounding of Yugoslavia under communist dictator Tito, who baptized the young state with blood by massacring around 90,000 political opponents (especially royalists and fascists).[259] Tito would seek to steer a middle course between the communism of the Soviet Union and that of the capitalist West. Most importantly, under Tito, Yugoslavia was ruled systematically multi-ethnically to ensure that no nationality or ethnicity would dominate any political sector or public institution.[260]

Yet memories of the WWII mass violence remained alive, if officially suppressed by Tito. When Tito died in 1980, ethnic-nationalism resurgent, politically and ethnically motivated writers re-examined WWII, its different ethnic victims in Yugoslavia, and charged each other with genocide.[261] Notable among these was Croatian president-to-be Franjo Tuđman who, as anti-Semite and Croatian nationalist, had two reasons to minimize the Holocaust and anti-Serb killings during WWII.[262] Post-Tito discourses on genocide among the different ethnic groups in Yugoslavia, especially but not exclusively among Serbs, played a prominent role in the breakup of Yugoslavia. In its famous 1986 memorandum, the influential Serbian Academy of the Sciences and Arts, for example, read post-WWII Yugoslav history as discriminating and, in Kosovo as the historical heartland of the Serbs, committing genocide against the Serbs.[263]

When in Kosovo of 1981 anti-Serbian riots broke out accompanied by demands that Kosovo gain the full status of a federated entity—alongside Slovenia, Serbia, Bosnia and Herzegovina (hereafter I will use “Bosnia” to denote Bosnia-Herzegovina), Macedonia, Croatia, and Montenegro—Serbian nationalists were increasingly alarmed given that they considered Kosovo the historical heartland of Serbia, even if by now the ethnic majority was resoundingly Muslim Albanians (owing to emigration of Serbs and higher birthrates of Muslims).[264] Kosovo would prove to be a touchstone for the nationalists of the other constituent republics of Yugoslavia as well, since Kosovar demands of greater autonomy—Kosovo was then a so-called autonomous province of Serbia—mirrored growing Slovenian and Croat demands for independence from Yugoslavia. Tensions in and about Kosovo gave rise to a new, nationalist type of leadership pioneered first by Milošević, [265] and thereafter followed by his racist Croatian counterpart Tuđman (elected in 1990), his democratic but still Slovenia-centric colleague Milan Kučan (elected in 1990), and Bosnia’s Alija Izetbegović (Bosnian president from 1990 on) whose strong Islamic leanings impeded possibilities of his representing Bosnia’s non-Muslim populations—his uneasy coalition with Croats and Serbs in parliament soon facing open fractures—even if his politics were arguably less prone to producing violent outcomes as those of Tuđman and Milošević. Milošević’s street rallies and Serbian-centered rhetoric throughout Kosovo of the late 1980s troubled Slovenia and Croatia, and when in 1989 Milošević abrogated the autonomy of Kosovo and Vojvodina (and saw a compliant regime installed in Montenegro) an alarmed Croatia and Slovenia accelerated their independence efforts, [266] while sowing the seeds for the future war between Serbia and Kosovo in 1998-1999. Unlike the economically more powerful (and thus more independent) as well as ethnically less diverse republics of Slovenia (88% Slovenian, 2.8% Croat, 1.4% Muslim, 2.4% Serbian)[267] and Croatia (78.1% Croat, 12.2% Serbian),[268] the economically troubled and very multi-ethnic republic of Bosnia (43.77% ethnic Muslim also called “Bosniak”, 31.46% Serbian, 17.34% Croatian),[269] was at first more reluctant to assert independence claims. [270] But when it became clear that Slovenia and Croatia would secede, staying within a heavily Serb-dominated Yugoslavia was understandably unfathomable for Izetbegović’s voter base in Bosnia.[271]

Although nationalism and insufficient engagement with violent histories were certainly important causes of the increasingly rapid disintegration of Yugoslavia in the 1980s and ensured it would crack along the ethnic and territorial lines it eventually did in the 1990s, an economic downfall had already been mounting under the late Tito era and provided nationalist movements with social disillusionment and marginalization to exploit. The state took on ever larger amounts of debt and expended a growing percentage of its budget on paying interests while inflation began running rampant and unemployment, always high in Yugoslavia, never dipped below double digits from 1974 on and reached almost 17% in 1988.[272] Economic development and unemployment also differed widely between the different regions, further fueling nationalist tendencies. Thus, the least developed parts of Yugoslavia—Bosnia, Kosovo, Macedonia, and Montenegro—were all well above the national average in terms of unemployment and well exceeded 20% by 1990 (Kosovo with by far the worst rate was at over 38%), and employment afflicted the 19-24 years group the most.[273] Inflation was at 46% in 1981 and at 70% in 1985.[274] The IMF stepped in to provide loans conditional on market and political reform, but IMF performance here was in line with its at best ineffectual and at worst detrimental impact on developing economies more generally.[275]

In the first years of the 1990s, the situation came to a head politically and economically. In 1990 elections were held in all the Yugoslav republics with the result that only Serbia and Montenegro cast a decisive vote in favor of remaining within Yugoslavia and maintaining a pro-communist government.[276] Croatia and Slovenia, the most economically powerful republics, refused tax payments to the federation while Serbia, in 1990, took out a massive unsecured loan from the federal bank amounting to 1.8 billion USD without the knowledge of the other republics that in turn reduced their contributions to the military budget. [277] The army thus saw its financial existence threatened by the secessionists whose striving towards independence also politically undermined the raison d’être of a Yugoslav army that in turn was already dominated in the rank-and-file as well as the officer corps by Serbs—the ethnic group already most interested in maintaining Yugoslavia. The army confiscated weaponry in Slovenia, Croatia, and Bosnia, purged its ranks, and began arming Serbian militias in Croatia and Bosnia. [278] Within Bosnia, Serbs were also decidedly overrepresented in the police and other security organs of the republic.[279] The tug-of-war after the 1990 Bosnian elections over assigning police posts based on ethnic criteria only amplified ethnocentric ideologies and ethnicity-based regionalization in the main organ able to use armed force.[280]

In 1991 Slovenia and Croatia proposed a confederation plan that would have made their secession legal, although also sought to preserve a reduced Yugoslavia in the style of the European Union, rather than a federation. This plan, however, was rejected by Serbia and Montenegro.[281] Slovenia and Croatia simultaneously declared their independence (backed by preceding referenda) in the same year (Macedonia would follow a few months later). Two days after this declaration, the Yugoslav army began a brief war with Slovenia that lasted for ten days and eventually assured Slovenia full independence.[282] By this point, the Serbian minority in Croatia anticipating the inevitability of secession but wishing to remain in Yugoslavia and fearing Tuđman’s only somewhat restrained sympathies for the historic and genocidal Ustaše—a fear only fanned by Serbian nationalist discourse with a proclivity to elevate any anti-Serb acts to the level of genocide—was already fighting a war against Croatia that led it to control about a third of Croatia’s territory (compared to their 12.2% share of the Croatian population) within seven months.[283]

The protracted and violent conflict in Croatia waged by a relatively small minority backed by the vast resources of the Yugoslav army was indicative of what would be in store for Bosnia where Serbs constituted more than twice the share of the population compared to in Croatia and where there was also a sizeable minority of Croats. Bosnia’s more powerful neighbors Croatia and Serbia had already convened and agreed in spring 1991 to carve up Bosnian territory for purposes of national expansion.[284] Despite their political power, Tuđman and Milošević were less decisive for the particular nature of the war that was to be fought in Bosnia as the leader of the Bosnian Serbs Radovan Karadžić and the generals that would wage it. Late in 1991, Karadžić declared (one among similar such remarks in other contexts) before the Bosnian parliament: “Do not think you will not lead Bosnia and Herzegovina into hell and the Muslim people into possible annihilation, as the Muslim people cannot defend themselves in case of war here”.[285] Such statements made before the onset of military conflict and the drafting or implementation of concrete genocidal policy should not be read as inevitably concluding in genocide (similar remarks by Hitler about the extermination of Jews before the German parliament in 1939 has also led to etiologically simplistic historical judgments).[286] Nonetheless, it is clear that the leading political figure of the Bosnian Serbs regarded a potential war not simply as prosecuting positive, political interests for his side—say, the preservation of Yugoslav unity or the incorporation of Bosnian Serbs into Serbia—but rather as prosecuting the extinction of the other side. Then, in 1992, as Bosnian Serbs proclaimed regional autonomy in the parts of Bosnia where they constituted a majority, the Bosnian parliament moved to issue an independence referendum. Without the Serbs who abstained, the referendum decided, with 99.4% of the votes cast in favor that made up 62.7% of the eligible voter population (including Serbs), secession from Yugoslavia.[287] A contemporaneous attempt by European powers and the US to seek a non-militant resolution that sought, very much in agreement with especially Bosnian-Serbian plans, an regional division of Bosnia along ethnic lines came to naught when Izetbegović withdrew his half-hearted agreement.[288] War was inevitable, and in April 1992 Bosnian-Serbs, just as their compatriots had done in Slovenia and Croatia, began shelling Sarajevo and Zvornik. Within weeks they held 60% of Bosnia’s territory.[289] Things got worse when Croats, who previously had supported the Izetbegović government, decide to use the opportunity to capture land themselves and soon controlled 20% of Bosnian territory.[290]

Over the following three years, the fortunes of war and alliances between Croats and Bosnian-Serbs as well as Bosnian Muslims and Croats shifted while peace plans failed. Serbia was hit by UN sanctions and peacekeepers were sent to curtail at least the worst excesses.[291] One area where UN peacekeepers were active was the town of Srebrenica which the local general, the Frenchman Philippe Morillon, declared a safe area. Bosnian-Serbs encircled and besieged the region in 1995 and ever larger groups of Muslim refugees needed to be housed in the town that was in increasingly dire need of humanitarian assistance. The Muslim forces there plundered surrounding Serbian villages in search for food and resources, leading to several thousand Serbs fleeing the area,[292] which was then propagandistically exploited by Bosnian Serb forces wishing to exact revenge on the Muslims in Srebrenica. Eventually the Bosnian-Serb forces led by general Ratko Mladić conquered the enclave, proceeded to separate women and children from military-age boys and men, and deported the former from Srebrenica while exterminating the latter through shooting, resulting in 8,000 deaths. Mladić and his political leader Karadžić were judged to have committed genocide in the case of Srebrenica—as well as numerous other crimes throughout the war—when they were later prosecuted by the International Tribunal of the Former Yugoslavia for their actions.[293] The massacre and further Serbian shelling of civilians in Sarajevo led to NATO aggressive interventions (primarily via air) that, together with a NATO-supported large offensive in Croatia (Operation Storm) which finished Serbian hopes of achieving any military objectives there and resulted in mass expulsions or flight of Serbian civilians, forced Serbia to accept peace conditions that were agreeable to the other negotiating parties. These so-called Dayton Accords divided Bosnia into heavily federalized structure wherein Bosnian Serbs were given considerable autonomy and gave international actors considerable political and military powers to intervene to secure peace and democratic transition. These accords are typically viewed as successful and seem to enjoy, among each of the Bosnian ethnic groups, more support than not, nonetheless the top-down imposition of memory and justice as well as the accords’ entrenching of ethnic regionalization have also found some critics.[294] Decades later, however, peace holds and further wars seem unlikely (albeit the Kosovo war 1995-1999 destabilized the region before it was ended with NATO intervention), despite an initially 2.21 million displaced persons and a third of all housing having been destroyed. [295] Yet, obstacles to returning for those who fled or were cleansed are still substantial and many formerly diverse regions, not least in Bosnian-Serb republic and former Serbian majority parts of Croatia, never regained the ethnic diversity they once had[296]—of the one million returnees, only 44% were minority returns, well over a million was still awaiting return in 2011.[297]

The case of the Bosnian genocide poses challenges we have not been confronted with in any of the other cases of genocide discussed so far. The Bosnian genocide in the narrow and legal sense of the term is confined to the murder of over 8,000 boys and men at Srebrenica in 1995 that constitutes the largest civilian massacre in this civil war and on European soil since the Holocaust. Of these victims more than 6,600 have been genetically identified (by 2016) in what may well be the most forensically documented atrocity to date.[298]  Nonetheless, the genocide at Srebrenica can neither historically nor representationally be investigated separately from the Bosnian civil war in which it took place. Muslims, Croats, and Serbs all fought one another at one point or other during this war and religious ethno-nationalism was rife on all sides (although there was also prominent civilian dissent against nationalism, not least in Serbia).[299] No atrocities were committed by Jews against Germans during WWII. Even in cases were a genocide victim group was a party to a war which fought the genocidal perpetrator group, for example when the Herero and Nama were fighting the Germans, any atrocities against German civilians by the Herero and Nama pale in comparison to German deeds. The same claims cannot be made in the Bosnian war where atrocities were committed by all war parties, even if those committed by the Serbs exceed the scale of the others. Every side in the war had concentration camps of varying severity and size in which were detained group members of the other warring parties in varying proportions,[300] even if the Serbian camp system got most of the headlines at the time and today remains the most well-known and most researched.[301] Ethnic cleansing—the practice of exerting force and violence on a group to remove them from a territory—was often associated only or at least dominantly with the Serbs, but was in fact a practice used by all sides. Among the largest single ethnic cleansing operations, although it took place in Croatia, yet was aided by Bosnian Muslim forces, was the Croat Operation Storm in 1995, which ended all hostilities by decisively putting down the Serb uprising in the country. Yet, flight and ethnic cleansing of Serbs during the operation resulted in up to 200,000 Serbs displaced, as well as at least 410 Serbian civilian deaths (the Serbian side today claims 1,211 civilian and, including combatants, 1,861 total dead).[302] Little attention was paid to this Serb suffering at the time. While an initial judgment at the International Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia (ICTY) found that indiscriminate shelling and other crimes with the intent to forcibly displace the Serbian population had taken place during the operation, the appeals judgment overturned these verdicts.[303]

At the same time, however, acknowledging the complexity of violence in the Bosnian war should not lead us to claim a false equivalence of atrocities, as do some relatively prominent voices in discourses on the Bosnian war. Despite numerous reports that the war cost 200,000 to 250,000 lives, we now know the war killed a total of about 97,901 with civilians accounting for 41%. Of these, 65.4% (64,036) were Bosnian Muslims which meant 3.4% of their population counted in the 1991 census died, a significantly higher proportion than any other ethnic group in Bosnia. Serbs suffered 24,905 deaths, 25.4% of the death toll and 1.8% of the 1991 Serb population, whereas for Croats the numbers were 8% (7,788) and 1%.[304] The proportion of deaths that were civilians were also much higher for Bosnian Muslims than the other groups. [305] Over two thirds of convictions at the International Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia are against Serbs. The largest, clearest, and most deliberate case of mass killing in this war was also committed by the Serbs when they executed 8,000 unarmed men and boys (more than the entire Serbian civilian death toll in the war)[306] in Srebrenica.

The main perpetrator of the Srebrenica genocide was the Bosnian-Serb army (VRS) supported by various police units (although a famous piece of video evidence also shows the paramilitary Scorpions group executing boys and men).[307] Yet, atrocities more generally during the Bosnian war were committed by a plethora of different groups. The main group responsible for the Serbian concentration camp system which served to terrorize and displace non-Serb populations through detainment, torture, humiliation, rape, and select, visceral killings were police units, though at times in cooperation with the army or, more often, paramilitary groups.[308] Paramilitary groups played an elevated role for all sides, but rose to infamousness particularly for the Bosnian Serbs. The 56 paramilitary groups fighting for the Serbs and totaling between 20,000 and 40,000 men dwarfed the 13 and 14 groups fighting for the Croatian and Bosnian Muslim sides, respectively.[309] Professional, “disciplined” paramilitary groups like the Bosnian-Serb Tigers and Scorpions used anti-civilian violence instrumentally in order to remove non-Serbs either by killing or by violent expulsion.[310] Expressive violence that sought highly public display of exceptionally cruel violence, at times on select Islamic holidays, was committed by less professional groups such as the Yellow Wasps (subject of a documentary).[311] Likely partly owing to the relatively large number of professional military or paramilitary, the most common Serb violence against civilians seems to have been instrumental violence in order to conquer territory, whereas Bosnian Muslim violence was typically retaliatory and in response to prior Serbian violence.[312]

Motives for violence against civilians were similarly manifold (as is common for genocidal violence in general). Paramilitary leaders like Vojislav Šešelj, who led the ultra-rightwing Serbian Radical Party in Serbia, were undoubtedly predominantly driven by rabid nationalism, which can also be said of a number of other perpetrators. Given the economic troubles and high unemployment in Yugoslavia, economic motives certainly constituted push and pull factors for participating in militant units. The infamous criminal and paramilitary leader Arkan, who led the “Tigers”, saw considerable financial gains from his paramilitary activities.[313] The prominence of ethnic cleansing in the war suggests the strategic value of this form of violence which could create an ethnically purer state to consolidate territorial gains and facilitate greater ease of post-war governance. Sexual violence for purposes of perpetrator sexual gratification, or also of terrorizing, torturing, dominating, and humiliating victims was common, particularly among Milan Lukić’s White Eagles (also known as Avengers).[314] This at times uneasy alliance of different perpetrator groups was led by an alliance of three main figurers. The Bosnian Serb army was led by Radovan Karadžić as the political representative of Bosnian Serbs and by Ratko Mladić, Karadžić’s subordinate and the main military commander of Bosnian Serb forces. Officially at arm’s length but crucial in terms of providing military materiel and financial support was Serbia’s leader Milošević. Yet, just as Milošević’s alliance with Serbian extremists like Šešelj could be uneasy—not least over over the latter’s rapacious territorial demands as a condition for peace deals[315]—so was his relationship with the two Bosnian-Serb leaders, not least over their use of anti-civilian violence. Consequently, unlike the responsibility of Karadžić and Mladić for the genocide in Srebrenica which is not in doubt, Milošević’s involvement in the genocide is contested, despite the court’s conclusion (in a ruling of two to one), that he was sufficiently aware of the genocidal intentions of his subordinates.[316] A final verdict in Milošević’s was never reached because he died before conclusion of the trial. Several mid and lower-ranking perpetrators have also been prosecuted and found guilty for genocide at the international court, which concluded its proceedings in 2017, but the bulk of such perpetrators—644 cases and 978 defendants—were tried at the local courts in Bosnia where cases continue even if at a slowed pace.[317]
